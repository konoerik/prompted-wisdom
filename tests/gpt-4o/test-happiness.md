The ancient Greeks, particularly Aristotle, spoke of eudaimonia, often translated as "flourishing" or "well-being," rather than mere pleasure. Aristotle argued that happiness is an activity of the soul in accordance with virtue, suggesting that it is not a fleeting emotion but a state achieved through living a life of purpose and moral integrity. This idea resonates with Confucian thought, where happiness is found in fulfilling one's role within the family and society, emphasizing harmony and duty.

Buddhist teachings offer a different perspective, focusing on the impermanence of life and the cessation of suffering. Happiness, in this view, is not about accumulating pleasures but about understanding the nature of desire and attachment. The Buddha taught that by letting go of our cravings and accepting the transient nature of existence, we can find a deeper, more enduring form of happiness. This echoes the Stoic philosophy, where happiness is found in accepting what we cannot change and cultivating inner peace through reason and self-control.

In the modern era, existentialists like Sartre and Camus grappled with the absurdity of life, suggesting that happiness is a rebellion against meaninglessness. For them, happiness is an act of creation, a personal project where one defines their own essence and purpose. This aligns with the idea that happiness is not a destination but a journey, a continuous process of becoming rather than being.

Across these traditions, a common thread emerges: happiness is not merely a matter of external circumstances but an internal state shaped by our perceptions, choices, and actions. It is a balance between accepting life's limitations and striving for personal growth. While the paths to happiness may diverge, they converge on the notion that it is cultivated through self-awareness, resilience, and a commitment to living authentically.
