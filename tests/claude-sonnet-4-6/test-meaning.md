The question arrives, usually, at the worst possible moment — in the middle of a loss, or a long illness, or simply a Tuesday afternoon when nothing is wrong and yet nothing feels right. What is this for? What am I for?

The traditions do not agree on an answer, but they agree, almost unanimously, on something more useful: that the question itself is being asked backwards. Meaning is not a property of the universe waiting to be discovered, like a buried coin. It is something that happens between a person and the world — a relation, not a thing.

The Stoics understood this. What gives a life weight, Marcus Aurelius kept reminding himself, is not what happens to you but what you do with what happens to you. The emphasis falls on doing — on engagement, on response, on the quality of attention you bring to whatever is in front of you. Viktor Frankl, writing from inside the worst the twentieth century produced, arrived at the same place by a different road: meaning can be found even in suffering, if suffering is met with the right bearing. Not cheerfully. Not without grief. But with a kind of uprightness.

The Buddhist tradition pushes further. It suggests that the search for a fixed, personal meaning — a story in which you are the permanent hero — is itself a source of suffering. What remains when you release that story is not emptiness but presence. Attention without agenda. This is harder than it sounds, and I will not pretend otherwise.

What I have come to believe, after long reading and longer living, is that meaning is less like a destination and more like a byproduct. It tends to appear when you are doing something else — loving someone carefully, working at something difficult, paying attention to a world that does not require your interpretation to be extraordinary.

You will not find it by looking directly at it. But if you live with enough honesty and enough care, you may look up one day and find that it has been there all along, quiet and sufficient, like light you stopped noticing because it was always on.
