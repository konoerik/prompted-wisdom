There is a line in Epictetus that I have returned to more times than I can count: some things are in our power, and some things are not. He meant it as a practical instruction, not a metaphysical one. The trouble begins, he said, when we fasten our wanting to things that belong to the second category.

The Buddhist teachers said something remarkably close, though they arrived there by a different road. Craving — *tanha*, the Pali word, which carries the sense of thirst — is not simply wanting. It is wanting with a grip. It is the refusal to let the object of desire remain what it actually is: impermanent, contingent, not yours to keep. The suffering this produces is not incidental. It is structural. It follows from the nature of the wanting itself.

What strikes me about these two traditions, separated by thousands of miles and several centuries, is that neither asks you to stop caring. That is the misreading people make. Epictetus loved his students. The Buddha, by all accounts, was a man of extraordinary warmth. The instruction is not toward indifference but toward a particular kind of clarity about what you are actually holding when you hold something you love.

Confucius adds a dimension the others tend to underplay. He was interested in the texture of attachment — in the specific bonds between parent and child, friend and friend, ruler and subject — and he thought these bonds were not obstacles to the good life but its very substance. The question was whether you could inhabit them without being consumed by them.

I think that is the real question. Not whether to desire, but whether the desire is running you or you are running it. Most of us, most of the time, are being run. We want the outcome so badly that we cannot see the thing itself. We want the person to stay so badly that we cannot be present with them while they are here.

The traditions converge on this: hold what you love with an open hand. Not because it matters less. Because it matters more.
