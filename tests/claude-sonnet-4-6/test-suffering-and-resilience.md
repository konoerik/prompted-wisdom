Nobody gets through without it. That is the first thing to say, and it is worth saying plainly before anything else, because one of the cruelest tricks the mind plays is to convince you that your suffering is exceptional — that others have been spared what you are carrying. They have not. The traditions agree on almost nothing else as completely as they agree on this.

The Buddhists made it their starting point. The first noble truth is simply that suffering exists, that it is woven into the fabric of a life, not a deviation from it. There is something almost relieving in that directness. You are not broken. You are alive.

The Stoics arrived at a similar place by a different road. Epictetus, who had been a slave and knew something about conditions he could not change, kept returning to a single distinction: what is in your power and what is not. Almost everything that causes suffering, he noticed, falls into the second category. The loss, the illness, the betrayal — these arrive without your permission. What remains yours is how you meet them. This is not a counsel of passivity. It is a counsel of precision: stop spending yourself on what you cannot move, and turn your full weight toward what you can.

Marcus Aurelius, writing to himself in the small hours, put it differently: *The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way.* I have tested that line against hard years, and I believe it. Not because suffering is secretly good, but because the person who comes through it — who does not look away, who does not collapse into bitterness — is genuinely changed by the passage.

What the traditions diverge on is whether that change is the point. The Buddhists want you to loosen your grip on the self that suffers. The Stoics want you to strengthen it. I am not sure these are as different as they sound. Both are asking you to stop being a passive recipient of what happens to you.

That, I think, is what resilience actually means. Not the absence of damage. The refusal of helplessness.
