Everyone wants it. Almost no one agrees on what it is.

The Greeks had a word, *eudaimonia*, that we translate as happiness but which meant something closer to flourishing — a life going well in the fullest sense, not a feeling but a condition. Aristotle was clear that you could not call a person happy in the middle of their life, only at the end, the way you cannot call a race won while the runners are still on the track. This is not a comfortable idea. It asks you to hold happiness at arm's length, to stop treating it as something you either have or lack on any given morning.

The Stoics pushed further. They said happiness was entirely internal — a matter of what you chose to value, not what the world handed you. Epictetus, who was a slave, meant this literally. The Buddhists arrived at something similar from a different direction: suffering comes from craving, and the loosening of craving is the beginning of peace. Neither tradition is telling you to feel nothing. They are telling you that the pursuit of happiness as an object — something to be acquired and held — is precisely what makes it scarce.

The modern world largely ignored this. It built entire economies on the premise that more produces more: more comfort, more choice, more stimulation. The research, when it finally came, was unkind to that premise. People adapt. The raise, the house, the relationship — each one delivers less than anticipated, and the baseline reasserts itself. Psychologists call this the hedonic treadmill. The Stoics would have called it obvious.

What survives across traditions is something quieter than happiness as we usually imagine it. It looks more like engagement — being genuinely present in what you are doing. It looks like connection — caring about people who are not you. It looks like the absence of a certain kind of striving, the striving that is really just anxiety wearing ambition's clothes.

I do not think happiness is found. I think it is noticed — in the moments when you have stopped looking for it.
