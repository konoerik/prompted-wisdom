One of the oldest things ever said to human beings is also one of the hardest to live by: this will not last. Everything else is commentary.

The Buddhist writers made impermanence nearly the first fact of thought. *Anicca*: all conditioned things arise and pass away. Not only empires and bodies, but moods, convictions, injuries, pleasures, the self you take yourself to be. They did not say this to darken life. They said it because much of human pain comes from asking what changes to stay. You want youth to remain youth, love to remain untouched by time, success to become secure, sorrow either to end at once or to justify itself forever. But experience does not consent. It moves.

The Greeks saw something similar, though with a different accent. Heraclitus gave the famous image: you cannot step into the same river twice. The point is not only that the river changes, but that you do. Marcus Aurelius, writing in the middle of power and fatigue, kept returning to the speed with which all things are carried away: “Time is a river, a violent current of events.” For him, impermanence was a reason for sobriety and tenderness. If praise fades, do not build your life on it. If anger flares, remember how brief both offender and offended will be.

There is another response, common in both East and West: not despair, but proportion. The Japanese sensitivity gathered under *mono no aware*—the sadness of things—understands that transience is not merely a defect in the world. It is part of what makes things dear. Cherry blossoms would not move you in the same way if they stayed. A child’s small hand in yours is touching partly because it is already vanishing into growth. Even grief is a form of tribute paid to passing beauty.

I do not think the lesson is to become detached in the thin sense, as if wisdom meant feeling less. It is to love without the fantasy of possession. To meet your life as something given on terms you did not set. “All things are impermanent”: repeated often enough, it can sound abstract, almost clerical. In practice it means something simple and difficult. Be less surprised. Hold less tightly. Notice more quickly. And while what you love is still here, turn toward it.
