The first time I read the *Diamond Sutra*, I was struck by how little it cared for permanence. The Buddha speaks of a raft that carries you across the river, then must be left behind. Not because it was useless, but because its use was always temporary. The same is true of everything we cling to: bodies, beliefs, even the self. The sutra does not argue against attachment out of moralism, but out of simple observation. Things change. To resist this is to suffer.

Marcus Aurelius, writing centuries later in his *Meditations*, puts it differently but no less plainly: "All that you see will soon perish; those who witness the perishing will soon perish themselves." He is not being grim, only honest. The Stoics saw impermanence as a fact to be faced, not a tragedy to be lamented. If everything is passing, then what matters is how we meet that passing—whether with fear or with dignity.

In the *Tao Te Ching*, Lao Tzu writes: "The hard and stiff will be broken. The soft and supple will prevail." Here, impermanence is not just a truth but a strategy. Water wears away stone not by force, but by persistence. The lesson is not to harden yourself against change, but to move with it.

What unites these traditions is not a shared vocabulary, but a shared refusal to lie. They do not promise that impermanence can be escaped, only that it can be endured—and, if you are lucky, understood. The Buddhist monk Thich Nhat Hanh once said that to be born is to be given a precious gift: a chance to wake up. Waking up, in this sense, means seeing the world as it is, not as we wish it to be.

I have found that the people who struggle least with impermanence are not those who deny it, but those who have made peace with it. They do not cling to youth, to certainty, to the illusion of control. They know that every moment is borrowed, and so they treat it with care. This is not resignation, but a kind of freedom. If nothing lasts, then nothing is owed to the past, and nothing is guaranteed in the future. All that remains is the present—and how you choose to meet it.
