You will spend much of your life wanting things you do not have and fearing the loss of things you do. This is not a flaw in your character. It is the condition.

The Buddha saw this clearly twenty-five centuries ago. He called it *tanha* — thirst, craving — and he taught that it is the root of suffering. You grasp at pleasure, at security, at permanence, and because nothing lasts, you suffer. The solution he offered was radical: let go. Not of life itself, but of the clinging. Learn to hold things lightly, to want without grasping, to love without possessing.

The Stoics arrived at something similar from a different direction. Epictetus divided the world into two categories: what is up to you and what is not. Your desires, he said, should align only with what you control — your own judgments, your responses, your character. Everything else — wealth, reputation, even the lives of those you love — belongs to fortune. To desire what fortune controls is to make yourself a slave.

I have found both teachings true, and both incomplete. The Buddha is right that attachment breeds suffering, but some attachments are worth the suffering. You will love people who will die. You will care about work that may fail. To renounce all attachment is to renounce being human.

The Stoics are right that you must distinguish what you control from what you do not, but the line is not as clean as Epictetus suggests. You do not fully control your desires, and you are not indifferent to outcomes. You can practice equanimity without pretending you are made of stone.

What I have come to believe is this: desire is not the enemy, but unconscious desire is. The wanting that runs you, that you have never examined, that makes you frantic and small — that is what binds you. But desire that you have looked at, that you have chosen, that you hold with open hands — that can be a form of freedom. You will still lose what you love. You will still fail to get what you want. But you will not be owned by it.
