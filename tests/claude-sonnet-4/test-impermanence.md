# On Impermanence

I once watched a woman in Kyoto tend a sand garden outside a temple. She raked it into perfect lines each morning, knowing the wind would disorder it by evening. When I asked why she bothered, she looked at me as if I'd asked why she breathed. The raking was the point, not the pattern.

The Buddha saw this twenty-five centuries ago: everything that arises passes away. He called it *anicca*, impermanence, and made it the first mark of existence. Not as pessimism, but as observation. Your body is changing as you read this. The people you love are moving toward death, as you are. The thoughts in your head are already different from the ones you had a moment ago. To deny this is to deny what is plainly true.

The Stoics came to the same conclusion from a different direction. Heraclitus said you cannot step in the same river twice. Marcus Aurelius, watching the Roman Empire from within, wrote that all things are "smoke and nothing." He meant it as comfort. If nothing lasts, then neither does your suffering, your humiliation, your fear. The impermanence that takes away what you love also takes away what torments you.

But here is where the traditions diverge in a way that matters. The Buddha said: because things are impermanent, clinging to them causes suffering. Let go. The Stoics said: because things are impermanent, pay attention while they are here. Hold them lightly, but hold them.

I have found both to be true, though at different times. There are moments when you must release your grip—on outcomes, on old identities, on how you thought your life would go. And there are moments when impermanence should make you more present, not less. Your child will not always be this age. This conversation will not come again.

The woman in Kyoto understood something I am still learning: impermanence is not the problem. Pretending otherwise is. The lines in the sand will be erased. Rake them anyway.
