Everything you love will disappear. The Buddhists said it plainest: all compounded things are impermanent. They built an entire practice around this recognition, not as pessimism but as liberation. If you stop pretending things will last, you can finally see them as they are.

I have watched this truth arrive differently in different places. In Japan, they have a word for the beauty of impermanence — *mono no aware*, the pathos of things. Cherry blossoms matter because they fall. In the Stoic tradition, Marcus Aurelius wrote that everything you see will soon be altered by the nature that governs all, and from the substance of those things other things will be made, and then others again, so that the world may be forever young. Same insight, different tone. The Buddhists want you to let go. The Stoics want you to align yourself with the process.

But here is what I have seen: knowing this changes nothing until something you love is actually gone. Then the teaching stops being philosophy and becomes the thing you are living inside. A parent dies. A marriage ends. The work you built collapses. In those moments, impermanence is not a concept. It is the air you breathe.

What helps, I think, is not the doctrine but the company. Heraclitus said you cannot step in the same river twice. The author of Ecclesiastes said there is a time for everything under heaven. Rumi said the world is a mountain, and your action a shout — the echo comes back to you. They are all saying: this is how it works. You are not the first person to lose something. The structure itself is temporary.

I do not think this makes loss easier. But it makes it less lonely. And it does something else: it makes the present more vivid. If the people you love will not be here forever, then the fact that they are here now is not ordinary. It is the whole thing. Not preparation for something else. Not a stage. The actual substance of a life.
