Suffering comes to everyone, and the thinkers knew this before they knew anything else. The Buddha made it his first truth. The Stoics built their entire philosophy around it. What separates the traditions is not whether suffering exists, but what you should do when it arrives.

The Buddha said suffering comes from wanting things to be other than they are. You cling to what passes, you grasp at what cannot be held, and the clinging itself becomes the pain. His answer was to loosen your grip—not to stop caring, but to stop demanding that the world conform to your will. I have found this true in small ways and large. The suffering of a delayed train dissolves when you stop insisting it should have been on time. The suffering of loss remains, but it changes shape when you stop fighting the fact that everything ends.

The Stoics came at it differently. Epictetus said to distinguish what is up to you from what is not. You cannot control what happens, only how you meet it. Marcus Aurelius, writing to himself in a military tent, said that the obstacle becomes the way—that the very thing blocking you can become the means of moving forward. This is not optimism. It is something harder and more useful: the recognition that your response is the only territory you govern.

Nietzsche went further. He said that suffering could be transformed into strength, that what does not kill you makes you stronger. I think he overstated it—some suffering only breaks people—but there is something in it. Resilience is not innate. It is built, and it is built through endurance.

What holds across all of them is this: suffering is not a mistake in the design. It is the design. The question is not how to avoid it but how to stand in it without being destroyed. You do that by loosening your grip on what you cannot hold, by focusing on what remains in your control, and by remembering that endurance itself is a kind of victory. Not the one you wanted, perhaps. But the one that matters.
