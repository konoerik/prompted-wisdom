Happiness is not what you think it is when you first reach for it. The early mistake is to imagine it as a feeling you can capture and keep, a state you arrive at and remain in. Every tradition I have read warns against this. The Stoics said it plainly: if you make your happiness depend on things outside your control, you have already lost it. Epictetus told his students to distinguish between what is up to them and what is not, and to care only about the former. The Buddhists went further, arguing that the very grasping after pleasant feelings is what binds you to suffering. They called it *tanha*, thirst, and said it could never be satisfied.

But this does not mean happiness is impossible. It means you have been looking in the wrong place. Aristotle came closest to what I have found to be true. He said happiness—*eudaimonia*—is not a feeling but an activity, a way of living in accordance with virtue over the course of a whole life. You cannot be happy in a moment; you can only be happy in the way a tree is healthy, by growing well according to your nature.

The Confucians understood this too. They spoke of harmony, of finding your place in the order of things and fulfilling it with care. Happiness was not self-expression but self-cultivation, the patient work of becoming what you ought to be.

What holds up, then, is this: happiness comes from living well, not from feeling good. It comes from doing what is worth doing, from treating people as they deserve, from facing what must be faced without complaint. It is not a reward for virtue but the byproduct of it, the way warmth is the byproduct of a fire.

You will have moments of joy, and you will have long stretches where you feel nothing much at all. Neither tells you whether you are happy. Look instead at what you are doing, at who you are becoming. That is where happiness lives, if it lives anywhere.
