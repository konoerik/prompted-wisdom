Suffering is inevitable. That’s the first thing to understand. The Buddha said it plainly: life is suffering. Not that life is *only* suffering, but that suffering is woven into the fabric of existence. Loss, disappointment, illness, aging, death – these are not anomalies, but fundamental aspects of the human condition. To deny this is to set yourself up for constant frustration and disillusionment.

The Stoics, too, understood this. Epictetus, born a slave, knew suffering intimately. He taught that we cannot control what happens to us, but we can control how we respond. This is the key to resilience: not avoiding suffering, but transforming our relationship to it. "It's not what happens to you, but how you react to it that matters," he said.

But how do we transform that relationship? One way is through acceptance. Not passive resignation, but a clear-eyed recognition of reality. To fight against what is, is to fight against the universe itself, a battle we are destined to lose. Acceptance allows us to conserve our energy, to focus on what we *can* control.

Another way is through meaning. Viktor Frankl, a psychiatrist who survived the Holocaust, observed that those who found meaning in their suffering were more likely to survive. Meaning provides a framework, a purpose that transcends the immediate pain. It gives us something to hold onto when everything else is stripped away.

I have found that both acceptance and meaning are essential. Acceptance allows me to acknowledge the reality of my suffering, while meaning gives me the strength to endure it. It's a constant practice, a daily effort to reframe my experiences, to find the lessons hidden within the pain. It is not easy, but it is possible. And in that possibility lies the hope for resilience.
