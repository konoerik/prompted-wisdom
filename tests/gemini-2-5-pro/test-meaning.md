I have come to believe the question itself is a trap. When you ask, “What is the meaning of life?” you are asking for a secret, a final answer hidden somewhere outside of yourself. It sends you searching for a treasure that was never buried. The thinkers who have felt truest to me suggest the question must be turned inward. It is not what you can expect from life, but what life expects from you. The challenge, as Viktor Frankl saw it from the depths of a concentration camp, is to answer for your own life, to find your meaning in response to its demands.

This is a harder path, for it offers no single destination. The existentialists, like Camus, faced an indifferent universe and concluded that we must create meaning through our rebellion against that indifference. He asks us to imagine Sisyphus, condemned to push his boulder up a hill for eternity, as happy. His meaning is found not in the completion of his task, which is impossible, but in the struggle itself. This is a stark but honest view. It finds a strange echo in the Stoic court of Marcus Aurelius, who saw meaning not in some grand cosmic purpose, but in the simple, daily fulfillment of one’s nature. “What is not good for the hive,” he wrote to himself, “is not good for the bee.” Your meaning is braided together with the good of the whole.

These ideas, born worlds apart, seem to converge on a single, solid point. Meaning is not something you find; it is something you forge. It is not a revelation; it is a responsibility. It is built, moment by moment, in the work you choose, the love you give, the suffering you bear with courage, and the part you play in your own small hive. It is the resonance of a life lived in response to its demands.
