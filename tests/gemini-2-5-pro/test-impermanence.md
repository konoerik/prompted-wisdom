You have felt it, I am sure, in moments of quiet reflection: the sense that everything is slipping through your fingers. The face in an old photograph is a ghost. The city you grew up in exists only in memory. This is not a private sadness, but the oldest truth. The Buddha built a whole system of thought on this single observation: that all things are impermanent. He taught that our suffering arises not from the fact of change, but from our desperate attempt to cling to what must pass.

Centuries earlier, in a different world, the Greek Heraclitus said the same: “You cannot step into the same river twice.” The river is not the same, and neither are you. The Roman Stoics took this idea and turned it into a practical tool. When faced with loss, ambition, or fear, Marcus Aurelius would remind himself that all is “ephemeral, the memory of it and the thing itself.” He found that seeing life as a brief procession of dissolving forms could shrink his anxieties to their proper size. I have found this to be a severe but effective medicine.

But this truth is not only a tool for grim resilience. The Japanese have a term, *mono no aware*, which points to the gentle sadness and profound beauty of transient things. It is the feeling of watching cherry blossoms scatter in the wind, their loveliness inseparable from their brief existence. This, I think, is the key. To fight impermanence is to declare war on reality itself. To accept it is not to surrender, but to see clearly. The only thing that does not change is the fact of change itself. This is the anchor. Not a point of stillness in a shifting world, but the understanding that the world is *all* shift, and that you, too, are part of its ceaseless, heartbreaking, beautiful flow.
