Of all the truths the old thinkers left us, this one is the hardest to live with: nothing lasts. We build our lives on the illusion of solidity, but the ground is always shifting. The Greek Heraclitus put it most famously: you cannot step into the same river twice, for other waters are ever flowing on to you. The person who steps in is also different a moment later. This idea of constant flux, *panta rhei*, is not just a clever riddle; it is the fundamental nature of things.

Centuries later and half a world away, the Buddha built a whole path on this insight. He called it *anicca*, or impermanence. Our suffering, he taught, is the friction of our desire for permanence against the grain of a changing world. We try to grasp the water of the river, to hold onto a feeling, a person, a moment of triumph, as if it were a stone. But it is not a stone. And the pain of our clenched fist is a pain of our own making.

The Roman emperor Marcus Aurelius took this truth as a daily medicine. In his *Meditations*, he reminds himself that all things are “ephemeral and cheap,” that Alexander the Great and his mule driver are now one and the same in the earth. He did this not to be morbid, but to gain perspective. If all this is fleeting—fame, wealth, power, even life itself—then what truly matters? It is a tool for calming the soul, for seeing past the urgent but trivial dramas of the day.

But it is not only a harsh lesson. The Japanese have a term, *mono no aware*, for the particular beauty of transient things. It is the gentle sadness and deep appreciation one feels watching the cherry blossoms fall, knowing their beauty is inseparable from their briefness.

These traditions do not offer you a way to halt the river. They agree that the struggle itself is the problem. The anchor they offer is the insight that you are not standing on the bank, trying to hold back the water. You *are* the water. The goal is not to stop the flow, but to stop fighting it.
