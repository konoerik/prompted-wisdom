The first thing to understand about happiness is that you will likely never find it if you go looking for it. John Stuart Mill put it best: “Ask yourself whether you are happy, and you cease to be so.” The great thinkers, almost without exception, saw happiness not as a goal to be pursued but as a consequence of a life lived well. The modern hunt for it, as a feeling to be captured and sustained, would have struck them as a profound mistake.

The Greeks had a better word for it: *eudaimonia*, which is not a feeling but a state of flourishing. For Aristotle, this was the result of an activity, the practice of living a life in accordance with virtue. It is something you *do*, day by day, over the course of a whole life, not something you *have*. The Stoics took this idea of practice and turned it inward. They taught that tranquility, the only reliable form of happiness, comes from distinguishing what is in your power from what is not, and concerning yourself only with the former. Your health, your reputation, the actions of others—these are ultimately beyond your command. Your own judgments, your intentions, your will—these are yours alone. As Marcus Aurelius wrote, in a line I have carried with me for years, “The happiness of your life depends upon the quality of your thoughts.”

This is where I find the Buddhist thinkers so bracing. They go a step further, suggesting that the very craving for happiness is a root of suffering. By focusing on the release from attachment, they point toward a peace that is deeper than any fleeting joy. What I have found, sifting through these traditions, is that they all converge on a single, difficult truth. Happiness is not a prize to be won. It is a byproduct, found not at the destination but in the living of the life itself.
