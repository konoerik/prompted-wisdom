You feel it the moment you wake: a wanting. It is the engine that moves you through the day, the pull toward food, warmth, companionship, recognition. Without it, you would not get out of bed. And yet, I have found that this same force is the source of nearly all your grief. The wanting is not the problem; the attachment is. The Buddha was bluntest about this, identifying a fundamental “thirst” or craving as the origin of our suffering. We suffer because we desperately want things to be a certain way, and we suffer again when, having gotten them, we live in fear of them changing.

The Stoics, half a world away, arrived at the same place. They saw that our peace is ruined not by events themselves, but by our passionate desire for specific outcomes we cannot control. Epictetus put it in a line I have carried for years: “Do not seek for things to happen the way you want them to; rather, wish that what happens happen the way it happens: then you will be happy.” This can sound like a call to be passionless, a stone. I have not found it to be so. Epicurus, for his part, suggested a gentler path: not to extinguish desire, but to curate it. He argued for satisfying the simple, natural desires—for friends, for thought, for freedom from pain—and abandoning the empty, insatiable ones for wealth and fame.

What I have found is this: the work is not to stop wanting, which is impossible, but to change the nature of your wanting. It is to learn the difference between enjoying a thing and needing it for your peace. The joy of a sunlit afternoon is real, but the frantic demand that tomorrow be just as sunny is a recipe for misery. The goal is not to feel nothing, but to learn to hold the world, and all you love in it, with an open hand. What comes, you receive. What goes, you let go. The space in between is a kind of freedom.
