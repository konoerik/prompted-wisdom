There is a difference between happiness and meaning, and it matters. You can be happy without meaning — a warm afternoon, a cold drink, nothing pressing. And you can have meaning without happiness — ask anyone who has spent years caring for someone they love through illness. The two overlap, but they are not the same thing, and confusing them will cost you.

Happiness is a state. Meaning is a direction.

Frankl saw this more clearly than most, because he saw it from inside a place designed to strip both away. What he found in the camps was that the people who survived were not the strongest or the luckiest but the ones who still had a reason — a task unfinished, a person waiting, something that needed them specifically. "He who has a why to live," he wrote, borrowing from Nietzsche, "can bear almost any how." That line has been repeated so often it has gone soft, but it was forged in a place that was not soft at all.

The Stoics arrived at something similar by a different road. For them, meaning came from fulfilling your role — your function, they would say — within a larger order. Marcus Aurelius compared a person who acts without reference to that larger order to a severed hand: still shaped like a hand, but no longer one. You become what you are by being part of something.

The Buddhist tradition is warier of this. Meaning, like everything else, can become a thing you cling to, and clinging is the root of suffering. But even here there is the bodhisattva vow — the commitment to remain in the world for the sake of all beings. That is not the absence of meaning. That is meaning held lightly.

I think the honest answer is this: meaning is not found the way you find a lost key. It is made, the way you make a promise — by binding yourself to something beyond your own comfort. It does not need to be grand. Camus imagined Sisyphus happy, but I think he imagined him something more important than happy. He imagined him engaged. The rock was his.

You will not find meaning by thinking about meaning. You will find it by forgetting to look, because your hands are already full.
