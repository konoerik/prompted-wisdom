Most people, if you ask them what they want, will eventually arrive at the word happiness. But press them on what they mean and the answers fracture. Pleasure, contentment, joy, peace, flourishing — these are not the same thing, and the confusion between them has caused more misery than almost any philosophical error I can name.

Aristotle saw this clearly. He used the word *eudaimonia*, which we translate as happiness but which really means something closer to living well and doing well across a whole life. It was not a feeling for him. It was an activity — the soul operating in accordance with its highest capacities. You could not call a person happy on the strength of a single afternoon. You had to see the shape of the entire life. "One swallow does not make a summer," he wrote, "nor does one day."

The Epicureans are routinely misunderstood. Epicurus did not teach indulgence. He taught that pleasure, rightly understood, is the absence of pain in the body and trouble in the mind. His actual life was remarkably simple — bread, water, a garden, conversation with friends. He found that most of what people chase in the name of happiness actively destroys their peace. The wanting is the problem, not the having.

The Buddhists arrived at something strikingly similar by a different road. The second noble truth locates the origin of suffering in craving — *tanha*, that restless thirst that moves from object to object and is never finally satisfied. Happiness, in this framework, is not something you acquire. It is what remains when you stop grasping.

I have come to think that the traditions converge on a point most people would rather not hear: happiness is not the reward for getting what you want. It is closer to a byproduct of how you meet what comes. Montaigne put it with characteristic ease — the greatest thing in the world is to know how to belong to yourself.

If there is a practical lesson in all of this, it is simple and difficult. Stop trying to feel happy. Try instead to live in a way you would not be ashamed of. The feeling, when it comes, will come unbidden.
