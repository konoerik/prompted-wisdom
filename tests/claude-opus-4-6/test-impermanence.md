Everything changes. That much is obvious. But the traditions differ — sharply, consequentially — on what to do with that fact.

The Buddhist teaching is the most unflinching. *Anicca*, impermanence, is not a regrettable feature of existence but its very nature. Nothing you can point to — a feeling, a body, a civilization — holds its form. The suffering comes not from the change itself but from the grip, the insistence that something should stay. "You are afraid of losing what you have," the *Dhammapada* might say, "but you never had it." To see impermanence clearly is, in that tradition, the beginning of liberation.

The Greek response is different in texture but not entirely in substance. Heraclitus saw the same river and the same flux, but he found in it not a call to release but a hidden order — the *logos* that persists through change. The Stoics inherited this. For Marcus Aurelius, meditating constantly on how all things pass was not an exercise in detachment for its own sake but a way of clarifying what matters. If the marble will crack and the empire will fall, then your character — how you meet this moment — is the only thing that is genuinely yours. Impermanence, for the Stoics, was a tool for moral focus.

Montaigne, centuries later, took a gentler line. He did not try to master impermanence or transcend it. He simply watched himself change — his tastes, his opinions, his body — and found in that honest observation a kind of freedom. "I do not portray being," he wrote. "I portray passing." There is something deeply humane in that. He did not need a system. He needed a mirror.

Where these traditions converge is here: the refusal to look at impermanence honestly is what distorts a life. Whether you call it *dukkha* or anxiety or self-deception, the pattern is the same — a person building on sand and then raging at the tide.

I think the practical lesson is not to stop caring. It is to care with open hands. To love what is here precisely because it will not stay. That is not resignation. It is the only realism that does not curdle into bitterness.
