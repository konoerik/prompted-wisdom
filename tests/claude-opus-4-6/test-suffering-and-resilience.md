You will suffer. That much is not in question. The only question worth asking is what you will do with it — whether it will merely be something that happened to you, or whether it will become something you can use.

The Buddha placed suffering at the very foundation of his teaching. Life is dukkha — unsatisfactoriness, pain, the ache of things not being what we want them to be. This was not pessimism. It was a diagnosis, offered because he believed a cure existed. The cause is craving, the clinging to what cannot stay. Release the grip and the suffering loosens. Twenty-five centuries later, that observation still cuts.

The Stoics arrived at something remarkably similar by a completely different road. Epictetus, himself a former slave, taught that it is not things that disturb us but our judgments about things. You lose something precious. The loss is real. But the torment you add to the loss — the conviction that it should not have happened, that the world has been unfair — that is your own contribution. Marcus Aurelius, writing to himself in a military tent on the frozen Danube, put it with Roman brevity: "Choose not to be harmed — and you won't feel harmed."

I have sat with that line many times and I will tell you honestly: it is both true and incomplete. There are harms you do not choose — the death of a child, systemic cruelty, the slow dismantling of a body by illness. To tell someone in the grip of such things that they are merely judging poorly is not wisdom but coldness. Nietzsche understood this. He did not counsel detachment. He said the goal was amor fati — to love your fate, all of it, including the worst. Not to tolerate suffering but to say yes to it as the price of being fully alive. That is a harder teaching, and I am not sure anyone can live it completely.

What I have found is this. Suffering is not redeemed by meaning. But meaning can be built from suffering, the way a wall can be built from broken stone. Viktor Frankl, who lost nearly everything in the camps, wrote that those who survived often had something ahead of them — a task, a person, an unfinished piece of work. Not an explanation for the pain, but a reason to endure it.

You cannot choose what will break you. You can choose what you will become afterward. That is not everything. But it is not nothing.
