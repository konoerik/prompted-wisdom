There is a story the Stoics liked to tell about a man who asked the philosopher Epictetus how to live well. Epictetus answered: “First, tell me what you want to be free from.” The man said he wanted to be free from fear, from anger, from the pain of loss. Epictetus nodded. “Then you must learn to want only what is within your power to control.” The man, confused, asked what that meant. Epictetus said: “It means you must stop wanting other people to be different than they are.”

This is the first truth about relationships: they are the place where we most often forget what we can and cannot control. We cannot control how others feel, what they think, or how they will act. We cannot make someone love us, or stay, or change. And yet we spend much of our lives trying. We bargain, we plead, we rage, we withdraw—all in the hope that if we just say the right thing, or do the right thing, or become the right kind of person, the other will finally give us what we need. But the other is not a machine. They are a living, breathing, unpredictable human being, just as we are. To demand that they be otherwise is to set ourselves up for suffering.

This does not mean we should not care. It means we must care differently. The Buddhist teacher Pema Chödrön writes: “To be fully alive, fully human, and completely awake is to be continually thrown out of the nest.” Relationships are the nest we are thrown from again and again. They force us to confront the limits of our control, and in doing so, they teach us something about love. Real love is not the feeling of being completed by another. It is the willingness to meet them where they are, even when where they are is not where we want them to be.

The Greeks had two words for love: <em>eros</em> and <em>agape</em>. <em>Eros</em> is the love that desires, that reaches out, that wants to possess. It is the love of the lover, the parent, the friend who cannot bear to be apart. <em>Agape</em> is different. It is the love that gives without expecting anything in return. It is the love of the stranger, the enemy, the person who has nothing to offer us. The Christian tradition made much of this distinction, but it appears in other forms too. The Confucian ideal of <em>ren</em>—often translated as “benevolence” or “humaneness”—is a love that begins with the self and extends outward, not because others deserve it, but because it is the right way to be in the world. The Stoics called it <em>oikeiosis</em>, the natural affinity we feel for others, not because they are useful to us, but because we recognize in them the same struggles, the same fears, the same longing for connection.

What all these traditions agree on is this: love is not a feeling. It is a practice. It is something we do, not something we fall into. The feeling of love—the rush of affection, the warmth of connection—is real, but it is not reliable. It comes and goes. What remains is the choice to act with kindness, with patience, with generosity, even when the feeling is absent. This is the love that lasts. This is the love that does not depend on the other’s behavior, or on our own moods, or on the circumstances of the moment. It is the love that says: <em>I will treat you with respect not because you have earned it, but because I have chosen to.</em>

Of course, this is easier said than done. We are creatures of habit, and our habits in relationships are often selfish. We want to be understood more than we want to understand. We want to be right more than we want to be close. We want the other to change so that we do not have to. But the moment we make love conditional—<em>I will love you if you love me back, if you stay, if you never hurt me</em>—we have already lost it. Love, in its truest form, is unconditional. Not because it is blind, but because it sees clearly. It sees the flaws, the wounds, the ways the other will fail us, and it chooses to stay anyway.

This does not mean we should tolerate abuse, or stay in relationships that drain us, or pretend that harm has not been done. Boundaries are not the opposite of love; they are a part of it. To love someone is to say: <em>I will not let you treat me in ways that diminish me, and I will not treat you in ways that diminish you.</em> This is the paradox of love: it requires both closeness and distance, both surrender and self-protection. The Sufi poet Rumi writes: “Your task is not to seek for love, but merely to seek and find all the barriers within yourself that you have built against it.” The barriers are not just in the other. They are in us. Our fear of vulnerability, our need for control, our refusal to forgive—these are the walls that keep love out.

And what of the love that ends? The relationships that break, the friendships that fade, the loves that die? Here, too, the old wisdom holds. The Stoics remind us that nothing lasts forever, and that to cling to what is passing is to guarantee our own suffering. The Buddhists say that attachment is the root of all pain, and that to love without attachment is to love freely. But what does that mean in practice? It means loving someone while knowing that one day, they may leave. It means holding them lightly, not because you do not care, but because you do. It means saying: <em>I love you now, in this moment, and I will not waste this moment worrying about the next.</em>

This is the hardest lesson of all: that love and loss are not opposites. They are two sides of the same coin. To love is to risk losing. To lose is to have loved. There is no way around this. The only question is whether we will let the fear of loss keep us from loving at all.

I have known people who spent their lives waiting for the perfect love, the perfect relationship, the perfect moment. They waited so long that they forgot how to love at all. And I have known people who loved fiercely, recklessly, without reservation—and who were broken by it. Neither path is wrong. Neither is right. What matters is that we choose. That we do not let fear make the choice for us.

You will love. You will be loved. You will lose. You will be lost. This is the way of things. The only thing you can control is how you meet it. Will you meet it with bitterness, with clinging, with the belief that you are owed something? Or will you meet it with openness, with gratitude, with the knowledge that even the pain of love is better than the emptiness of never having loved at all?

The answer is not in the other person. It is in you.
