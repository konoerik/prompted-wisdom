There is a story the Stoics liked to tell about Diogenes the Cynic, who lived in a barrel and owned nothing but a cloak, a staff, and a cup. One day he saw a child drinking from his hands and threw the cup away. The point was not that possessions are evil, but that we mistake them for necessities. The same could be said of society. We mistake it for a necessity, when in truth it is more like the cup—useful, even indispensable at times, but not the thing itself.

Most of us are born into a web of expectations, roles, and obligations before we have the chance to ask what any of it is for. We are given names, families, nations, religions, and by the time we are old enough to question them, they have already shaped us. The question is not whether to live in society—only a fool or a saint would try to live entirely outside it—but how to live within it without being consumed by it. The greatest thinkers, across traditions, have wrestled with this. Their answers are not always compatible, but they share a common thread: society is a tool, not a master. The danger is when we forget which is which.

Confucius, who lived in a time of warring states and collapsing order, saw society as a vast network of relationships, each with its own duties. The son must be filial, the ruler benevolent, the friend loyal. These were not arbitrary rules but the scaffolding of a harmonious life. "Let the ruler be a ruler, the subject a subject, the father a father, the son a son," he wrote. The Confucian vision is one of reciprocal obligation: you do your part, and in return, the world does its part for you. It is a vision of society as a kind of dance, where each step is prescribed but the whole is greater than the sum of its parts. The risk, of course, is that the dance becomes a cage. When Confucius was asked about governing, he said, "Guide them by edicts, keep them in line with punishments, and the people will stay out of trouble but have no sense of shame. Guide them by virtue, keep them in line with ritual, and they will have a sense of shame and become good." The difference is subtle but crucial. One approach treats people as problems to be managed; the other, as beings capable of something more.

The Stoics, writing centuries later in a very different world, took a harder line. Epictetus, a former slave, knew better than most how little control we have over the world around us. His advice was to focus on what we can control: our own judgments, desires, and actions. "Some things are within our power, others not," he wrote. "Within our power are opinion, pursuit, desire, aversion—in a word, whatever are our own actions. Not within our power are body, property, reputation, command—in a word, whatever are not our own actions." Society, for the Stoics, was one of those things outside our control. You could not choose your parents, your city, or the laws that governed you, but you could choose how to respond. The Stoic ideal was not withdrawal but engagement without attachment. You played your role—citizen, parent, worker—but you did not mistake the role for the self. Marcus Aurelius, the emperor-philosopher, put it plainly: "The soul becomes dyed with the color of its thoughts." Society will try to dye you in its colors. The question is whether you let it.

Buddhism, too, has something to say about society, though it is often misunderstood. The Buddha did not teach that society was evil, but that it was a source of suffering when we cling to it. The problem is not the world; the problem is our craving for it to be a certain way. The monk who renounces the world is not rejecting society so much as seeing it clearly. Thich Nhat Hanh, a modern teacher, wrote that "the way out is in." You do not need to leave society to find freedom; you need to see it differently. This is not so different from the Stoic idea of engagement without attachment. The difference is in the emphasis. The Stoics believed in duty; the Buddhists, in awareness. Both are right, in their way. Duty without awareness is blind; awareness without duty is empty.

Then there are the thinkers who saw society not as a dance or a cage but as a kind of organism. Hegel, writing in the early 19th century, argued that the individual is only fully realized through society. "The state is the actuality of the ethical idea," he wrote. It is not just a collection of individuals but a living thing, with its own logic and purpose. This is a more demanding vision than Confucius’s. For Hegel, society is not just a set of relationships but a force that shapes us, for better or worse. The individual who resists it entirely is not free but incomplete. The challenge is to find your place within the whole without losing yourself. This is easier said than done. History is full of societies that demanded too much—loyalty, conformity, even blood—and individuals who paid the price.

So where does this leave you? The answer depends on what you are willing to accept. If you want a simple rule, you will not find one. Society is too complex for that. But you can find a way to live within it without being swallowed by it. The Confucians would say: play your role well, but remember that the role is not the whole of you. The Stoics would say: do your duty, but do not mistake it for your identity. The Buddhists would say: see the world clearly, and do not cling to it. Hegel would say: find your place in the whole, but do not let the whole erase you.

I have found that the most useful approach is to treat society like a language. You did not invent it, but you can learn to speak it fluently. You can use it to express yourself, to connect with others, even to change it. But you do not have to believe that it is the only language, or the best one. You can borrow words from other traditions, other ways of being. You can even, if you are careful, invent a few of your own.

The danger is not society itself, but the belief that it is the only thing that matters. The greatest thinkers, across traditions, have warned against this. They have seen what happens when people mistake the cup for the water, the role for the self, the society for the soul. The challenge is to live in the world without being of it—to use society without being used by it. This is not a matter of withdrawal or rebellion, but of clarity. You do not have to leave the city to find freedom. You only have to remember that the city is not the only place to stand.
