The first thing to say about time is that it is not what we think it is. We speak of it as a river, a line, a container—something that carries us forward, something we can measure, something we can lose. But the river does not flow; the line does not stretch. Time is not a thing at all, but the way we experience change. The Greeks called it <em>chronos</em>, the steady, indifferent march of hours, but they also knew <em>kairos</em>—the right moment, the opportune time, the instant when something shifts and nothing is the same again. One is the clock; the other is the crack in the ice. Both are real, but only one leaves a mark.

Marcus Aurelius, who ruled an empire and wrote his meditations in the saddle between battles, saw time as a kind of mercy. “The universe is change,” he wrote, “our life is what our thoughts make it.” He meant that the present is all we ever have, and that to resist its passing is to resist life itself. The Stoics taught that we should hold our days lightly, as a man holds a bird—firm enough to feel its warmth, loose enough to let it fly. This is not resignation; it is clarity. The alternative is to spend your life clutching at what cannot be held, and in the end, the clutching is what breaks you.

The Buddhists go further. They say that our suffering comes from clinging to things as if they were permanent, when in truth everything is impermanent. The Sanskrit word is <em>anicca</em>—the understanding that all conditioned things are subject to decay. A flower blooms, a body ages, a thought arises and fades. To resist this is to fight the nature of reality. The Zen master Dōgen put it plainly: “To study the Way is to study the self. To study the self is to forget the self. To forget the self is to be enlightened by all things.” What he meant was that when you stop trying to freeze time, you begin to see it clearly. The moment you accept that everything passes, you are free to meet it as it is.

But there is another side to this. The Roman poet Horace, who lived in a time of civil war and knew how fragile life could be, wrote: <em>Carpe diem, quam minimum credula postero</em>—“Seize the day, trusting as little as possible in the next.” This is not the same as the Stoic or Buddhist view. It is not about detachment; it is about urgency. Horace was not telling us to let go of desire, but to direct it toward what matters now, because now is all we can be sure of. The future is a story we tell ourselves, and stories have a way of unraveling.

I have often wondered which of these is truer—the call to let go or the call to seize. The answer, I think, is that both are necessary, and the tension between them is where life happens. You cannot live well if you are always grasping, but you cannot live at all if you never reach. The trick is to hold the tension without letting it tear you apart. This is what the ancient Egyptians understood when they weighed the heart of the dead against a feather. The feather was <em>ma’at</em>—truth, justice, cosmic order. The heart was the record of a life. If it was too heavy with regret or greed, it was devoured. If it was light, it passed into eternity. They were not judging the weight of the sins, but the weight of the attachment to them. A life well lived was one that had learned to let go.

Mortality sharpens everything. When you know you will die, the question of how to spend your time becomes urgent. The medieval Christian mystics called this <em>memento mori</em>—remember you must die. It was not a morbid thought, but a clarifying one. If you keep death in mind, you will not waste your days on things that do not matter. The Japanese have a similar idea: <em>mono no aware</em>, the pathos of things, the bittersweet awareness of their transience. A cherry blossom is beautiful precisely because it will not last. To love it is to accept its passing.

But here is the paradox: the more you accept that you will die, the more alive you become. This is not a theory; it is something you can test. Try it. Spend a day as if it were your last. Not in a frenzy of pleasure, but with attention. Notice the light on the wall, the sound of a voice, the weight of your own breath. You will find that the things you thought were urgent—the emails, the arguments, the small anxieties—lose their grip. What remains is what matters: the people you love, the work that feels like yours, the quiet moments when you feel most yourself.

The trouble is that we forget. We get caught up in the illusion that time is something we can control, that we can hoard it, that we can bend it to our will. We make plans as if we were immortal, and then we are surprised when the plans fail. The truth is that time is not yours to control. It is yours to meet. The only power you have is in how you spend the moments you are given.

I once read a story about a man who asked a dying friend what he regretted most. The friend said, “I wish I had let myself be happier.” Not that he had failed to achieve, or to love, or to live fully—but that he had spent so much time worrying about the wrong things. Happiness, in the end, is not a destination but a way of traveling. The same is true of time. You cannot save it, or spend it, or waste it in any final sense. You can only choose how to meet it.

The last thing to say about time is that it is not the enemy. It is the medium in which we live. The Stoics were right: the present is all we ever have. The Buddhists were right: everything passes. Horace was right: the future is uncertain. But none of this means that time is meaningless. It means the opposite. Because time is short, every moment is precious. Because everything passes, every moment is unique. Because the future is uncertain, the present is all the more real.

You will die. This is not a threat; it is a fact. And it is the fact that gives life its shape. Without it, nothing would matter. With it, everything does. The question is not how to escape time, but how to live within it. The answer is not in the past or the future, but in the now—the only place where life is actually happening. You are here. This is it. What will you do with it?
