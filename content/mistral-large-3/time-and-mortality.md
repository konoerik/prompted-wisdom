There is a story the Stoics tell about a man who asks the philosopher how to live. The philosopher replies, *Memento mori*—remember you must die. The man is shocked, even offended. But the philosopher only smiles. He has not said it to wound, but to wake. Death is not the enemy of life; it is its sharpest teacher. Without it, the hours would stretch like water, formless and without weight. With it, every moment becomes a choice: to waste or to use, to forget or to remember.

This is not a new thought. The ancient Egyptians carved it into tombs: *I was nothing, I became, I am no more.* The Bhagavad Gita speaks of the self as unborn and undying, yet the body as a garment to be cast off. Marcus Aurelius, writing in his private journal, reminds himself that the universe is change, and that to resist this is to resist the nature of things. *You are a little soul carrying around a corpse,* Epictetus says, not unkindly. The corpse is not you. But it is yours to carry, for now.

What does it mean to remember you must die? It does not mean to live in fear, or to rush through days as if they were obstacles to be overcome. It means to see time clearly. Not as a line stretching endlessly ahead, but as a series of thresholds, each one narrower than the last. The Buddhists call this *anicca*—impermanence. Everything that arises passes away. The breath you just took is already gone. The person you were ten years ago is a stranger. Even the mountains, which seem eternal, are worn down by wind and rain. To forget this is to live in illusion. To remember it is to live in truth.

But truth is not always comfortable. There is a reason we forget. The mind recoils from the thought of its own ending. It invents stories: that we will live forever, that our work will outlast us, that our children will carry something of us forward. These are not lies, exactly. They are consolations. And yet, if we cling to them too tightly, they become chains. The Roman poet Horace wrote, *Carpe diem*—seize the day. But what does that mean? Not to grab at pleasure, but to recognize that the day is all we have. The past is memory, the future is imagination. Only the present is real.

This is where the traditions diverge. Some say time is an illusion, a veil over something eternal. Others say it is the only thing that is real. The Christian mystic Meister Eckhart writes that the soul is not in time, but time is in the soul. The Zen master Dogen says that time is being itself—each moment a complete world, containing all that has been and all that will be. What they share is the insistence that we are not separate from time. We are made of it. To resist it is to resist ourselves.

And yet, we do resist. We fill our days with noise, with distraction, with the endless scroll of things that do not matter. We act as if we have all the time in the world, when in truth, we have very little. The clock is always running. The question is not whether we will die, but how we will live while we are here.

There is a practice in some Buddhist traditions called *maranasati*—mindfulness of death. It is not morbid. It is a way of sharpening attention. If you knew you had only a year left, how would you spend it? A month? A day? The answer is not always what you think. You might assume you would chase pleasure, but often, the opposite happens. You begin to see what truly matters. The people you love. The work that feels like an offering. The quiet moments—the first light through the window, the taste of coffee, the sound of rain—that you have spent a lifetime overlooking.

This is the paradox of mortality: it is the thing we fear most, and the thing that gives life its shape. Without it, we would drift. With it, we are forced to choose. To say yes to some things and no to others. To let go of what does not serve us. To forgive, because time is short. To love, because it is the only thing that outlasts us.

I have known people who faced death with terror, and others who faced it with peace. The difference was not in the circumstances, but in how they had lived. Those who had spent their lives chasing things that did not matter—wealth, status, the approval of others—found little comfort at the end. Those who had loved, who had created, who had paid attention, carried that with them. They did not fear the dark. They had already lived in the light.

This is not to say that suffering is not real. It is. The loss of those we love, the decay of the body, the knowledge that everything we build will one day crumble—these are hard truths. But they are not the only truths. There is also this: that we are here, now, alive in a world that is vast and strange and beautiful. That we have been given the gift of consciousness, however briefly. That we can choose, in each moment, how to meet it.

The Stoics say that death is nothing to us, because when we are, death is not, and when death is, we are not. This is not a denial of grief, but a reminder of perspective. The dead do not suffer. Only the living do. And so the question is not how to escape death, but how to live in its shadow.

I do not know what comes after. No one does. But I know this: the fact of our mortality is not a curse. It is a compass. It points us toward what matters. Toward love, toward meaning, toward the things that endure not because they are eternal, but because they are true.

You will die. So will I. So will everyone you know. This is not a reason for despair. It is a reason to wake up. To look around. To say what needs saying. To do what needs doing. To hold what is precious while it is still here.

The clock is running. But the time is yours.
