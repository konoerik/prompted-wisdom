**A Letter to You**

I have spent most of my life reading the words of people who thought deeply about how to live. Some of them were wise, some were wrong, and many were both at once. What I have tried to do in these pages is not to repeat their answers, but to point you toward the questions that matter—the ones that do not go away, no matter how many books you read or how many years pass. You will have to answer them for yourself. But before you close this book, there is one last thing I want to say.

You are not a problem to be solved. You are a life to be lived. The greatest mistake I see people make—one I have made myself—is treating existence as a puzzle, as if there were a single right way to arrange the pieces. There isn’t. There is only the way you arrange them, day by day, with the materials you have. Some of those materials are given to you: your body, your time, the people you love, the suffering you cannot avoid. Others you choose: the work you do, the habits you form, the stories you tell yourself about who you are. The challenge is not to find the perfect configuration, but to keep arranging and rearranging, to stay open to the possibility that what seemed like a flaw yesterday might be the very thing that saves you tomorrow.

You will fail. You will disappoint yourself and others. You will lose things you cannot replace. This is not a sign that you are doing it wrong. It is the price of being alive. The people who seem to have it all figured out are usually the ones who have stopped asking questions. Do not envy them. Better to be uncertain and awake than certain and asleep.

Remember that most of the things you worry about will never happen, and the things that do happen will be things you never could have predicted. This is not cause for despair. It is cause for curiosity. What will you do with the time you have? Not the time you wish you had, or the time you imagine other people have, but the time that is actually yours—this hour, this day, this year? Will you spend it chasing things that do not last, or will you spend it on what matters? The answer is not a once-and-for-all decision. It is a series of small, daily choices, most of which you will not even notice until you look back.

Above all, be kind—to yourself and to others. Not because kindness is a virtue (though it is), but because it is the only response that makes sense in a world where we are all fragile, all temporary, all trying our best with the tools we have. You do not have to be perfect. You do not have to be right. You only have to be here, fully, for as long as you are given.

That is enough. More than enough. Now go.
