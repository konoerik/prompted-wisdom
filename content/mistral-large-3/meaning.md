On Meaning

You have probably asked yourself, at some quiet hour, what it all amounts to. Not in the grand, cosmic sense—though that question has its place—but in the smaller, more pressing way: what makes the days worth living? The answer is not the same for everyone, and it changes over time. But if you look across the centuries, you’ll find that the people who thought hardest about meaning were not trying to solve a puzzle. They were trying to live.

The Stoics said meaning comes from what you can control. Epictetus put it plainly: “Happiness and freedom begin with a clear understanding of one principle: some things are within our control, and some things are not.” You cannot control the world, but you can control how you meet it. Meaning, then, is not something you find—it is something you make, by choosing how to respond. This is not a call to passivity. It is a call to responsibility. If you spend your life chasing what you cannot have, you will feel empty. If you spend it shaping what you can—your actions, your character, your attention—you will feel alive.

The Buddhists said meaning comes from seeing clearly. The world is full of suffering, but suffering is not the problem. The problem is clinging—to things, to people, to ideas of how life should be. Meaning, in this view, is not found in accumulation but in release. Not in grasping, but in letting go. This is not a rejection of life. It is a refusal to be deceived by it. When you stop mistaking temporary things for permanent ones, you stop demanding that life give you what it cannot. You begin to see what is already here.

The Confucians said meaning comes from relationship. You are not an island. You are a son, a daughter, a friend, a citizen. Meaning is not something you discover alone. It is something you build with others. Confucius did not speak of self-actualization. He spoke of duty, of ritual, of the small, daily acts that bind people together. A life well-lived is not one of grand gestures, but of steady, honest engagement with the people around you. This is not sentimental. It is practical. The meaning of your life is not yours alone to decide. It is also shaped by how you treat others, and how they treat you in return.

The existentialists said meaning comes from choice. Sartre wrote, “Man is condemned to be free.” There is no script, no predetermined path. You must invent your own. This is terrifying, but it is also liberating. If there is no meaning handed to you, then you cannot complain that life has failed to provide one. You must make it yourself. This does not mean meaning is arbitrary. It means it is yours to create. You are not a passive recipient of life. You are its author.

These answers seem different, but they share a common thread. Meaning is not something you find outside yourself. It is something you do. It is not a destination. It is a way of traveling.

I have spent years turning these ideas over in my mind, testing them against my own life. The Stoic answer holds up when things go wrong. The Buddhist answer holds up when things go right. The Confucian answer holds up in the ordinary, unglamorous moments. The existentialist answer holds up when you feel lost. None of them are complete on their own. But together, they form a kind of compass.

You do not need to choose one. You can take what you need from each. When you feel adrift, ask yourself: What can I control? What am I clinging to? Who am I responsible to? What am I choosing? These questions will not give you a single answer. But they will keep you moving.

Meaning is not a fixed point. It is a practice. It is not something you achieve. It is something you return to, again and again. The people who seem to have found it are not the ones who have solved the riddle. They are the ones who have stopped looking for a solution and started living the question.

You will not find meaning in a book. You will not find it in a philosophy. You will find it in the way you live. In the way you meet the day. In the way you treat the people around you. In the way you respond to what you cannot change. In the way you let go of what you cannot keep. In the way you choose, again and again, to begin.

This is not a call to perfection. It is a call to honesty. Meaning is not about being right. It is about being real. It is not about having all the answers. It is about asking the right questions.

You already know what they are. You have been asking them all your life. The only thing left is to live them.
