What does it mean to ask about meaning? The question itself is slippery. It can sound like a demand for justification—why anything at all?—or like a plea for direction—what am I supposed to do with my life? The first is a question for metaphysics, the second for ethics, and both have been answered in ways that satisfy some and leave others cold. But there is a third way to ask it, quieter and more persistent: not *why* or *what*, but *how*. How do we live in such a way that the question doesn’t devour us? How do we find, or make, enough meaning to keep going?

The oldest answers are the most durable. The Stoics, who were as practical as they were severe, said meaning was not something to be discovered but something to be done. Epictetus, who had been a slave before he was a philosopher, put it plainly: *You are not your body, not your possessions, not your reputation. You are your capacity to choose.* Meaning, then, is not a thing but an act—the act of aligning your will with what you can control and accepting what you cannot. This is not resignation; it is the opposite. It is the recognition that meaning is not given but made, moment by moment, in the way you meet the world. The Stoics were not interested in grand narratives. They were interested in how a person endures.

The Buddhists, too, saw meaning as something constructed, but they began from a different premise: that suffering arises from the illusion of permanence. The Buddha’s first noble truth is that life is dukkha—often translated as suffering, but better understood as unsatisfactoriness, the sense that things are not quite right. The cause of this, he said, is tanha, craving—the desire for things to be other than they are. Meaning, in this view, is not found in the objects of desire but in the cessation of desire itself. Not in what you want, but in the space between wanting and having. This is not nihilism. It is the opposite: a radical affirmation of the present, precisely because it is fleeting. The Zen master Dogen wrote, *To study the Way is to study the self. To study the self is to forget the self.* Meaning, then, is not something you possess but something you dissolve into.

These two traditions—one Greek, one Indian—seem to pull in opposite directions. The Stoics say: act. The Buddhists say: let go. But both agree that meaning is not a fixed point but a practice. And both agree that the search for meaning is not a search for answers but for a way of being. This is the first thing that holds up when tested against how people live: meaning is not a destination but a manner of travel.

The modern world has tried to make meaning a matter of belief. You must have faith in something—God, progress, the nation, the self. But belief is fragile. It can be shaken by doubt, by loss, by the simple passage of time. The thinkers who have lasted are those who did not ask us to believe but to do. Confucius, who was less interested in the afterlife than in how to live well in this one, said that meaning was found in li—ritual, or the proper way of doing things. Not grand gestures, but the small, repeated acts of attention: how you greet a neighbor, how you eat a meal, how you mourn a loss. Meaning, for Confucius, was not a matter of doctrine but of conduct. It was not something you professed but something you performed.

This is the second thing that holds up: meaning is not a matter of what you think but of what you do. The twentieth century gave us existentialism, which took this idea to its logical extreme. Sartre said that we are condemned to be free—that there is no meaning except the meaning we create. This can sound terrifying, but it is also liberating. If there is no inherent meaning, then there is no inherent failure. You are not falling short of some cosmic plan. You are simply making choices, and those choices are yours alone. Camus, who was less interested in freedom than in rebellion, put it this way: *The struggle itself toward the heights is enough to fill a man’s heart.* Meaning, for him, was not in the achievement but in the striving.

But striving for what? Here, the moderns diverge. Some say meaning is found in work, in building something that outlasts you. Others say it is found in love, in the connections that make life bearable. Still others say it is found in suffering, in the way pain carves out a space for something deeper. All of these answers have merit, but none of them are universal. What works for one person may not work for another. This is the third thing that holds up: meaning is not one thing but many, and it changes over time.

I have known people who found meaning in raising children, in tending a garden, in writing a book that no one will read. I have known others who found it in silence, in solitude, in the refusal to be consoled. The common thread is not the content but the commitment—the willingness to say, *This matters, even if nothing else does.* This is not a grand declaration. It is a quiet one. It is the decision to treat your life as if it has weight, even when you are not sure it does.

The mistake is to think that meaning must be something vast and unshakable. The truth is that meaning is often small and provisional. It is the cup of tea you share with a friend, the walk you take when you cannot sleep, the way you keep going even when you do not know why. It is not a single answer but a series of small, stubborn acts of attention.

The greatest thinkers did not give us meaning. They gave us tools. They showed us how to look, how to endure, how to begin again. The rest is up to you.
