If you read long enough, across enough centuries, one thing becomes hard to miss: almost no serious thinker believed that a human being could become fully human alone. They disagreed about politics, metaphysics, salvation, pleasure, duty. But again and again they returned to the same fact: your life is shaped, steadied, enlarged, and sometimes ruined by the people you bind yourself to.

Aristotle said that without friends no one would choose to live, even if he had all other goods. That seems to me not merely elegant but true. There are pleasures you can enjoy alone, achievements you can pursue alone, even certain kinds of peace you can find alone. But a life sealed off from affection, loyalty, tenderness, and recognition becomes thin. It may be efficient. It may even be impressive. It is not, in the full sense, a human life.

The old philosophers were often more clear-eyed about love than we are. We inherit a language of romance that tells you to look for completion, destiny, a person who will finally answer the riddle of yourself. Most older traditions are less intoxicated. They ask instead: what sort of bond helps two people live well? What kind of love makes you less foolish, less selfish, less frightened? What kind of attachment deepens reality rather than blinding you to it?

Aristotle’s distinction still helps. He thought friendships are formed for pleasure, for usefulness, or for the sake of the good. The first two are common and not contemptible. Some people delight you; some help you. But these bonds are unstable because they last only as long as the pleasure or advantage lasts. The rare friendship is between people who admire something good in one another and, by loving it, help sustain it. This is not a sentimental thought. It means that love is not just a feeling of warmth but a way of seeing and fostering what is best in another person.

Confucian thought adds something equally important: love is not merely inward feeling but expressed care, shaped by conduct. Affection that does not take form in attention, courtesy, reliability, and restraint is too vaporous to trust. You can say you love someone and still humiliate them, neglect them, or make your moods their burden. The Confucian tradition understood that relationships are built from repeated forms: showing up, listening properly, remembering what is due, speaking in a way the other person can bear. This can sound formal to modern ears, but form is often what keeps love from collapsing into selfishness. We do not only need sincerity from one another. We need consideration.

The Buddhist traditions, on the other hand, are unsparing about the suffering woven into attachment. They do not tell you not to love. They tell you to examine what you call love. Is it care, or is it clinging? Is it delight in another’s existence, or a demand that they reassure you, remain unchanged, never leave, never disappoint? Much of what passes for love is really appetite mixed with fear. We want to possess what cannot be possessed. We ask another person to protect us from loneliness, contingency, mortality. No one can do that. When they fail, as they must, love curdles into resentment.

This is one reason the line from Simone Weil has stayed with me: “Attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity.” To love someone is, first of all, to attend to them—to see them more clearly than your vanity or hunger would prefer. Not as an extension of yourself, not as a role in your drama, not as the custodian of your unmet needs, but as a real person: opaque in places, wounded in places, stubbornly separate. The separateness matters. Love does not abolish it. In some ways love is the art of honoring it.

That is why the greatest danger in relationships is not conflict but fantasy. You imagine you know who the other person is. You imagine they owe you fluency, gratitude, permanence. You imagine that because feeling is strong, understanding is deep. Usually it is not. Love needs interpretation, correction, apology, patience. It needs room for the fact that two inner worlds never fully coincide.

The Christian writers, at their best, saw another truth that survives translation even if you do not share their theology: love is bound up with forgiveness. Not limitless submission to cruelty, not the erasure of judgment, but the willingness to absorb some injury without turning every hurt into a final verdict. Any long relationship will confront you with the repeated small failures of another person’s nature, and of your own. If every disappointment is treated as revelation—as proof that the beloved is inadequate—then affection cannot outlast reality. To love anyone for long is to make peace with imperfection without ceasing to ask for honesty.

What, then, seems solid to me? Not the idea that there is one person meant for you. Not the idea that love justifies itself by intensity. Not the idea that good relationships are effortless. What holds up is simpler and harder. Choose people you can respect. Be someone another person can rely on. Do not confuse being wanted with being known. Do not ask love to remove your solitude; ask it to accompany it. Learn to give attention before advice, truth before comfort, and mercy before pride.

And when love comes—whether as friendship, kinship, or eros—receive it with gratitude and a certain sobriety. It is one of the great goods of life, but not because it rescues you from being human. Because it lets you be human with someone else.
