Time becomes real to most people in the same way: not as a concept, but as a wound. A parent dies. A child is born. Your face in the mirror changes faster than your mind does. You discover that there are things you meant to do “someday,” and then one day you understand that *someday* was never a date on the calendar. It was a charm against fear.

The philosophers have said many things about time, but on mortality they are often less divided than they first appear. They begin from different pictures of the world. The Stoics thought of nature as an ordered whole in which loss is lawful. Buddhists spoke of impermanence, *anicca*, not as a defect in things but as one of their basic conditions. The writers of Ecclesiastes looked at human striving and found it vapor: “Vanity of vanities; all is vanity.” Montaigne, turning death over in his mind with unusual steadiness, said that “to philosophize is to learn how to die.” Different metaphysics, same pressure: you are not here long, and whatever wisdom is worth having must be able to survive that fact.

I think one of the first useful truths is that your resistance to mortality distorts time before death ever arrives. If you cannot bear that things end, you will not really be present for them while they are here. You will clutch, postpone, sentimentalize. You will ask of each good hour not only that it be good, but that it reassure you against its own passing. It cannot do that. Nothing can.

The Buddhist writers saw this clearly. Suffering does not come only from pain; it comes from trying to pin the world in place. Because things change, we suffer loss. But because we demand that they not change, we add fear to loss, and bitterness to fear. This does not mean you should become cold or detached in the vulgar sense. It means that love and transience are not opposites. In some deep way, transience is the price of love being real at all. What could it mean to cherish something that could not be lost?

The Stoics arrive by a different road at a neighboring thought. Marcus Aurelius tells himself, again and again, to look steadily at the nature of things: generation, change, dissolution. “You could leave life right now,” he writes. “Let that determine what you do and say and think.” This can sound severe, almost inhuman, until you realize what he is trying to remove: not affection, but triviality. The reminder of death is meant to clear the air. Many things that agitate you lose their authority when held against the fact that your time is finite. Vanity, resentment, delay, the endless rehearsing of old injuries—death does not make these impossible, but it exposes them as expensive ways to spend a short life.

Still, there is a danger in speaking this way. “Remember you will die” can become a slogan of false urgency. People hear it and imagine they must live at a frantic pitch, converting every hour into achievement or experience. But this too is a misunderstanding of finitude. To know time is limited is not to treat every moment as a test. It is to stop behaving as though your life were an inexhaustible resource. There is no prize for wringing from it the maximum quantity of motion.

Here I think the older traditions are wiser than many modern ones. They are less impressed by busyness. Confucian thought, for all its social emphasis, carries a quiet respect for seasonality: there is a time for study, for service, for mourning, for withdrawal. Human life is not mastered by acceleration. It ripens, if it ripens at all, through attention and repetition. The question is not whether you can outrun time, but whether you can inhabit it honorably.

Christian writers, too, often distinguish between mere duration and rightly used time. Augustine is haunted by time in the *Confessions*—how strange it is, how hard even to describe—and yet what matters finally is not how long a life is, but what it is ordered toward. Secular readers can resist the theology and still keep the insight. A long life is not necessarily a deep one. A short life is not necessarily unfinished in the only sense that matters. We do not control length. We have some influence over shape.

What, then, survives the collision between philosophy and ordinary life? Not much in the way of comfort, if by comfort you mean exemption. You will die. So will everyone you love. Time will take from you by degrees and then all at once. No serious tradition denies this, and those that promise to make peace with death by argument alone are not to be trusted.

But something steadier than comfort is available. First: mortality is not a mistake in the design. It is the condition under which seriousness, tenderness, patience, and courage become possible. Second: the awareness of death is useful only if it returns you to life as it is being lived now—to this conversation, this meal, this work, this person before you. And third: the right response to finitude is neither denial nor panic, but consent. Not cheerful acceptance, not a pose of wisdom—just the hard-won willingness to say: this is what it is to be here.

Seneca wrote that it is not that we have a short time to live, but that we waste much of it. That is true, though a little too neat. We do waste much of it. We also lose much of it to confusion, obligation, illness, grief, and simple human limitation. So I would put it more gently. You will not use your time perfectly. No one does. The point is only to stop giving it away so casually to what does not matter, and to stop imagining that life will begin later.

It has already begun. It is underway now. And because it ends, it asks something of you.
