Twenty. It’s a strange age, isn’t it? Old enough to feel the weight of the world, young enough to still believe you can lift it. Everyone tells you to “find yourself,” as if you’re a misplaced set of keys. But what if you’re not lost? What if you’re just…unformed? I’ve walked a long time, seen a lot of faces, and I’ve come to believe that knowing yourself isn’t about finding something that’s already there. It’s about building it.

The ancient Greeks knew this. Socrates, that gadfly of Athens, spent his days questioning everyone he met, not to impart wisdom, but to provoke it. He believed that true knowledge resided within each person, waiting to be unearthed through relentless self-examination. He famously said that the unexamined life is not worth living. It sounds harsh, but what he meant, I think, is that a life lived without conscious awareness, without questioning your assumptions and motivations, is a life lived half-asleep.

Centuries later, the Stoics took up this thread. Marcus Aurelius, the Roman emperor who wrote his meditations in the midst of war, understood that external events are largely beyond our control. What we *can* control is our response to them. He urged himself, and by extension us, to focus on virtue, on living in accordance with reason and nature. But how do you know what is virtuous? How do you know what is reasonable? By looking inward. By understanding your own biases, your own weaknesses, your own capacity for both good and evil.

This isn’t a comfortable process. It requires honesty, brutal honesty sometimes. Montaigne, the French essayist, understood this better than most. He wrote about himself, his flaws, his contradictions, with unflinching candor. He didn’t pretend to be a sage or a moral exemplar. He simply tried to understand himself, and in doing so, he offered a mirror to his readers. He showed us that it’s okay to be imperfect, to be uncertain, to be a work in progress. In fact, he suggested that this is the only way to be truly human.

Spinoza, the Dutch philosopher, took a different approach. He believed that understanding ourselves meant understanding our emotions. He saw emotions not as irrational forces to be suppressed, but as natural responses to the world around us. He argued that by understanding the causes of our emotions, we could gain control over them. This isn’t about becoming emotionless, but about becoming more aware of the forces that drive our behavior. It’s about understanding why you react the way you do, and then choosing a more considered response.

Nietzsche, the German philosopher, challenged the very idea of a fixed self. He argued that we are constantly becoming, constantly evolving. He saw the self not as a static entity to be discovered, but as a dynamic process of self-creation. This can be daunting, but it’s also liberating. It means that you are not defined by your past, by your mistakes, by the expectations of others. You have the power to shape yourself, to create your own meaning.

Viktor Frankl, a psychiatrist who survived the Holocaust, found meaning in the face of unimaginable suffering. He observed that even in the most horrific circumstances, people retained the freedom to choose their attitude, to choose how they would respond. He called this “the last of the human freedoms.” He realized that meaning isn’t something that’s given to us, but something that we create. It’s found not in pleasure or success, but in service to something larger than ourselves.

So, what does all this mean for you, standing at the threshold of adulthood? It means that knowing yourself is a lifelong journey, not a destination. It means that it requires courage, honesty, and a willingness to confront your own limitations. It means that it’s not about finding the “real” you, but about creating the you that you want to be. It means that you have the power to shape your own life, to find your own meaning, to become the person you are meant to be. Don’t be afraid to question, to explore, to experiment. The world is waiting to be discovered, and so are you.
