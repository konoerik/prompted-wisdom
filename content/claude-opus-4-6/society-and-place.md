You did not choose to be born into the particular arrangement of people and customs and laws that surrounds you, and yet here you are, shaped by it in ways you will spend a lifetime only partly understanding. This is the starting condition. Every serious thinker about society has had to begin here — with the fact that we are, as Aristotle put it, political animals, creatures whose nature is completed only in community, and yet who are also, each of us, something irreducibly singular, capable of standing apart from the group and asking whether it deserves our loyalty.

The tension between those two truths is old and unresolved. Confucius built an entire vision of the good life around right relationship — the son to the father, the subject to the ruler, the friend to the friend — and insisted that virtue was not a private attainment but something expressed through the quality of your participation in the social fabric. To withdraw from that fabric was, for him, a kind of failure. "If we cannot associate with people," he asked, "with whom can we associate?" The Daoists pushed back almost immediately. Zhuangzi told stories of useless trees that survived because no one wanted to cut them down, of crooked men who outlived the straight and useful. His point was not that society is evil but that its definitions of worth are narrow and often deadly, and that the wise person keeps a certain distance from them.

I have come to think both positions contain something essential. You are formed by the world you live in — your language, your sense of what matters, even the emotions you feel most readily are not entirely your own inventions. To pretend otherwise, to imagine yourself a sovereign individual who owes nothing and needs nothing, is a fantasy that collapses the moment you get sick, or lonely, or try to raise a child. But the society that forms you is also capable of deforming you. It can teach you to want what will not satisfy you, to fear what does not threaten you, to hate people you have never met. Recognizing this is not cynicism. It is the beginning of a mature relationship with the world you inhabit.

The Stoics understood this double bind with particular clarity. Epictetus, who had been a slave, knew that society's judgments about your worth could be not just wrong but obscene. Yet he did not counsel withdrawal. He counseled a kind of inner sovereignty — the capacity to fulfill your roles, as citizen, as neighbor, as parent, while refusing to let those roles define the whole of what you are. Marcus Aurelius governed an empire while reminding himself, in private, that fame is meaningless and that the people whose approval he might crave would themselves be dead within a generation. He did his work anyway. That combination — engagement without delusion — strikes me as one of the most difficult and valuable things a person can achieve.

There is a simpler version of this idea that I think holds up across traditions: you owe the world your participation, but you do not owe it your agreement. Thoreau made this case in its sharpest form when he argued that a person of conscience must sometimes refuse to cooperate with injustice, even at personal cost. But the same impulse appears in the Buddhist concept of right action, which asks you to act within the world while remaining unattached to the world's approval, and in Montaigne's quiet insistence that he could serve as mayor of Bordeaux without letting the office seep into his soul. "The mayor and Montaigne have always been two," he wrote, "with a very clear separation."

That separation is worth cultivating, not because society is your enemy but because it is powerful and you are permeable. The customs and opinions that surround you will enter you whether you invite them or not. The question is whether you will notice. Most people, most of the time, do not. They absorb the values of their place and period and experience them as their own deepest convictions. Socrates made himself unbearable to Athens by pointing this out, by asking people to give reasons for beliefs they had never examined. They killed him for it, which tells you something about how much societies value the examination.

I do not think the answer is to stand permanently outside. The answer, if there is one, is to stand at the edge — close enough to be useful, far enough to see clearly. To do your work, raise your children, pay attention to your neighbors, and vote your conscience, while maintaining, somewhere inside, a small quiet room that belongs to no one but you. Society will always ask for more than that. It will ask for your whole self. The wisest people I have read, and the wisest I have known, have found ways to love the world without handing themselves over to it entirely.
