I have been honest with you throughout this book, or I have tried to be, and so I want to be honest now about what I cannot do. I cannot hand you a life. I cannot even hand you a philosophy. What I have given you are pieces — things that struck me as true when I held them up against the way people actually live, things that survived that test when a great deal else did not. Whether they become anything more than pieces is not up to me.

Here is what I would want you to carry with you, if you carry anything at all.

You will forget most of what you have read here. That is fine. A book like this does not work the way a textbook works. It works more like weather — it passes over you, and what it changes, it changes quietly. If even one line has lodged somewhere in you, one thought that surfaces unbidden on a difficult morning, then the book has done more than I had any right to expect.

I wrote this because I spent a long time looking for solid ground and found that the ground is smaller than I hoped but more solid than I feared. The thinkers I have drawn on disagreed about nearly everything, but beneath the disagreements I kept finding the same few convictions, arrived at independently, across centuries and civilisations. Know what you are. Act with some courage and some care. Do not mistake comfort for happiness or pleasure for meaning. Pay attention to time, because it is the one thing you cannot argue with. Love others not because it is commanded but because the alternative is a kind of slow starvation. These are not exotic ideas. They are almost ordinary. But ordinary things are the hardest to hold onto, precisely because nothing forces you to notice them.

I want to say something about difficulty. The life these thinkers describe is not easy. Socrates was executed. Epictetus was a slave. Confucius wandered for years without a post. The Buddha left everything behind. They were not rewarded for their clarity — or rather, the reward was the clarity itself, and nothing else. If you take any of this seriously, it will cost you something. It may cost you a comfortable illusion, or a relationship that depends on you staying asleep, or a career that requires you not to ask certain questions. I cannot tell you the cost is worth it. I can tell you that every thinker I trust most would say the same thing: the unexamined life is the more expensive one. You just get the bill later.

Be gentle with yourself. Be honest with yourself. These two instructions sound like they conflict, but they do not. They are the same instruction, spoken in two different tones of voice.

Go well.
