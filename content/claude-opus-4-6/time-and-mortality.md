There is a line from Marcus Aurelius that I have never been able to set aside. He wrote it to himself, probably in a tent, probably tired: "Even if you were to live three thousand years, or thirty thousand, remember that no one loses any life other than the one being lived, and no one lives any other life than the one being lost." He was saying something precise. The past is gone. The future does not exist. What you have is this present moment, and it is the same size for everyone — the person who dies at thirty and the person who dies at ninety. Each of them can only lose now.

This is not a comfortable idea, but I have found it to be a sturdy one. Most of our suffering around time comes from the belief that we possess it — that the years ahead belong to us and can be stolen, or that the years behind us are a territory we might somehow revisit. Neither is true. You do not own your future any more than you own tomorrow's weather. And the past, as Heraclitus understood before almost anyone, is a river you have already stepped out of. The water that touched your foot is gone.

The Buddhists arrived at something remarkably similar through a different door. Impermanence — anicca — is not a problem to be solved but a feature of reality to be understood. Everything that arises passes away. Your body, your thoughts, the people you love, the civilizations that built the roads you drive on. The suffering comes not from the passing but from the grasping, the insistence that things should hold still when nothing does and nothing ever has. I think this is one of the most important insights any tradition has produced, and I notice that the people who have genuinely absorbed it tend to carry a certain lightness. Not indifference — lightness. They are freed to pay attention to what is here.

Seneca, who thought about time more carefully than almost any Western writer, made a distinction I keep returning to. Life is long enough, he said, if you know how to use it. The problem is not that we have a short time to live but that we waste a great deal of it. We give our hours to things that do not matter to us — to anxiety, to performance, to other people's expectations — and then complain at the end that life was brief. "You are living as if destined to live forever," he wrote. "Your own frailty never occurs to you." He was not being cruel. He was trying to wake his reader up.

What strikes me is how consistently the thinkers who faced mortality most directly became not grimmer but more vivid in their attention to life. Montaigne kept a skull on his desk and wrote the most life-loving essays in the French language. The Stoics meditated on death each morning and then went out to do their work with unusual focus and calm. The samurai tradition in Japan codified this into a principle — the way of the warrior is found in death, Yamamoto Tsunetomo wrote, meaning that only by accepting your death fully could you act with complete freedom in life. There is a paradox here, but it is a real one, confirmed by experience: the people who refuse to think about death are often the ones most paralyzed by it.

I want to be honest about what I have not resolved. Knowing that this moment is all you have does not make it easy to stay in it. The mind wanders forward and backward almost by reflex, and no amount of philosophy eliminates that tendency entirely. What the reading and the thinking have given me is not mastery but a kind of orientation — a reminder, when I catch myself living as though I have forever, that I do not. And that this is not a tragedy. It is the condition that makes anything matter at all.

Epicurus understood this. He argued that death is nothing to us, since where death is, we are not, and where we are, death is not. Some people find this cold. I find it clarifying. It does not eliminate grief — nothing eliminates grief, and nothing should. But it draws a clean line between the fear of death, which is a phantom, and the love of life, which is the real thing, the thing worth protecting.

You will lose everything. That is certain. The question the thinkers keep asking, across every tradition and century, is whether you will let that fact destroy your peace or whether you will let it teach you to pay attention. I have found no better question to sit with.
