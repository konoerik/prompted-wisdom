There is a line from Marcus Aurelius that I return to more than almost any other. He is writing to himself, as he always is, and he says: "Think of the life you have lived. You are afraid of tomorrow, but yesterday is just as irrevocable." It is a simple observation, but it cuts deep. We spend enormous energy fearing what has not yet happened while treating what has already passed as though it were somehow still available to us — as though we might go back and live it differently, or live it again. We cannot. The past is exactly as gone as the future is unwritten.

This is where most serious thinking about time begins: with the recognition that we do not have what we think we have. Seneca put it most sharply in *On the Shortness of Life*. Life is not short, he argued — we make it short. We squander it on things that do not matter, then complain at the end that there was not enough of it. "It is not that we have a short time to live, but that we waste a great deal of it." He was writing to a man named Paulinus, but he was writing to everyone, and the force of the argument has not diminished in two thousand years. Most people, if they are honest, will recognize themselves in it.

The Buddhist tradition arrives at something adjacent but distinct. Where the Stoics urge you to use your time well, the Buddhist teaching on impermanence — *anicca* — asks you to see that clinging to any moment, whether past or future, is itself the source of suffering. Everything arises and passes away. This is not a tragedy to be resisted but a truth to be understood. The Heart Sutra compresses it into a few dense lines: form is emptiness, emptiness is form. Nothing you can hold onto will stay in the shape you found it. The question is not how to make the most of your time but how to stop grasping at it as though it were yours to keep.

I think both of these are true, and they are true about different things. Seneca is right that we waste what we have. The Buddhist teachers are right that even what we do not waste will leave us. These are not contradictions. They are two honest descriptions of the same situation, seen from different angles.

Montaigne, who thought about death as much as anyone and more cheerfully than most, said that to philosophize is to learn to die. He borrowed the phrase from Cicero, who borrowed it from Plato, who put it in the mouth of Socrates. The idea traveled because it is sturdy. What Montaigne added was his own temperament — a willingness to sit with the fact of death without either dramatizing it or pretending it away. He kept death close, not as a morbid exercise but as a way of staying honest. "Let us rid it of its strangeness," he wrote. "Let us frequent it, let us get used to it." His argument was that the person who has come to terms with dying is freer than the person who has not — freer to enjoy what is here, freer to act without the paralysis that comes from pretending you have unlimited time.

Heidegger, centuries later, made a similar point in more difficult language. He called it *Sein-zum-Tode* — being-toward-death. His claim was that most of us live in a kind of evasion, acknowledging death abstractly while never letting it touch us concretely. Only when you truly face your own finitude, he argued, do you begin to live with what he called authenticity. I find the insight more useful than the vocabulary. The plain version is this: knowing you will die is not the same as *knowing* you will die.

What survives across all these traditions is a single uncomfortable fact dressed in different clothes. You have less time than you feel you have. Not because life is necessarily brief, but because the part of it you are actually present for — awake, undistracted, not deferring the thing that matters — is startlingly small. The Stoics knew this. The Buddhists knew this. Montaigne knew this. You probably know it too, in the way we know things we have not yet let change us.

I do not think the point is to live in constant awareness of death. That would be its own kind of distortion. The point is simpler and harder: to stop living as though the present moment were a rough draft you will get to revise later. You will not. This is the only version.
