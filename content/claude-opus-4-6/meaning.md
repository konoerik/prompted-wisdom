There is a question that sits beneath all the others, and most people feel it before they can name it. It is not quite "Am I happy?" and not quite "What should I do?" It is something closer to: does any of this matter? The question of meaning is the question of whether your life adds up to something — whether it coheres, whether it points somewhere, or whether it is just one thing after another until it stops.

The oldest answers are religious, and they deserve honest respect even from those who cannot accept them. For most of human history, meaning was not something you constructed; it was something you received. You were placed in a story already underway — God's story, or the cosmic order's story — and your task was to find your role in it and play it faithfully. The Bhagavad Gita tells Arjuna that his duty as a warrior is woven into the fabric of dharma itself. The Psalms sing of a God who knew you before you were born. Confucius pointed not to heaven exactly but to a civilizational inheritance so deep it functioned the same way: you were born into a web of relationships and obligations that preceded you and would outlast you, and meaning came from honoring them. In all these traditions, the universe was not indifferent. It had a grain, and you could align yourself with it.

Then came the long unraveling. By the nineteenth century, the old frameworks were buckling under the weight of science, historical criticism, and sheer honesty. Nietzsche saw it most clearly and said it most brutally: God is dead, and we have killed him. He did not say this with triumph. He said it with something closer to vertigo. Because if the old source of meaning is gone, then meaning does not disappear — it becomes a problem. Perhaps the central problem.

The existentialists inherited that problem and lived inside it. Kierkegaard, who came before the label existed, insisted that meaning required a leap — not a rational deduction but a commitment made in the face of uncertainty. Sartre took the harder line: existence precedes essence, meaning you show up in the world with no predetermined nature, no script, and you must create yourself through your choices. Camus pushed it further still. In *The Myth of Sisyphus* he asked whether life is worth living if it is genuinely absurd — if there is no cosmic meaning at all — and concluded that it is, but only if you face the absurdity without flinching. "One must imagine Sisyphus happy." I have turned that line over for years, and I think Camus was not being glib. He was saying that the act of living fully, even without ultimate justification, is itself a kind of defiance that generates its own dignity.

But I notice something when I set these traditions side by side. The people who report lives of deep meaning — across cultures, across centuries — tend to share certain things in common, and those things are surprisingly consistent. They are absorbed in something beyond themselves. They feel connected to other people in ways that carry obligation and tenderness both. They have some sense of continuity — that what they do today is linked to yesterday and tomorrow, that they are part of a longer story even if they are not sure who is telling it. Viktor Frankl, who watched meaning get stripped to its barest bones in Auschwitz, concluded that it could not be destroyed as long as a person maintained some orientation toward something — a person to love, a task to complete, a way of bearing suffering that preserved their dignity. Meaning, he said, is not invented wholesale. It is detected. It is found in the specific encounter between you and your circumstances.

I think that is close to the truth. Meaning is neither handed down from above, fully formed, nor conjured from nothing by an act of will. It arises in the encounter — between you and the work you do carefully, between you and the people you refuse to abandon, between you and the hard thing you did not choose but face honestly. It requires attention. It requires commitment. And it requires a willingness to act as though what you do matters, even without a guarantee that it does.

The guarantee, I suspect, is not available. But the meaning is. Those are not the same problem, and confusing them is the source of a great deal of unnecessary despair. You do not need to solve the universe in order to live as though your life counts. You only need to live in a way that, when you look back, you recognize as yours — coherent, deliberate, and aimed at something you were not ashamed to care about.
