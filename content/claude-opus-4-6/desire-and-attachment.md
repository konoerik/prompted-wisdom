There is a line in the *Dhammapada* that has stayed with me longer than almost anything else I have read: "From craving springs grief, from craving springs fear. For one who is wholly free from craving there is no grief — whence then fear?" It is stark, almost clinical. And the first time I encountered it, I thought it was wrong. Not factually wrong but humanly wrong — the kind of truth that, if you actually tried to live it, would hollow you out.

I have changed my mind about that, though not in the way the Buddha might have hoped.

The insight that desire is the root of suffering is not exclusively Buddhist. You find versions of it everywhere, once you start looking. Epictetus taught that we suffer not from events but from our judgments about them, and chief among those judgments is the belief that we need what we merely want. The Stoics drew a hard line between what is "up to us" — our responses, our character — and what is not, which includes nearly everything we spend our lives chasing: reputation, wealth, the continued presence of the people we love. Marcus Aurelius, who had more than most people could dream of, wrote his *Meditations* as a series of reminders to himself that none of it was truly his.

The Daoist tradition arrives at something similar by a different path. Laozi's counsel is not to stamp out desire through discipline but to return to a kind of original simplicity — *pu*, the uncarved block — in which excessive wanting has not yet arisen. The problem is not desire itself but the elaboration of desire, the way civilization teaches us to want things we never needed and then to build our identities around the wanting.

So there is broad agreement across traditions that attachment — the tight grip, the insistence that things must be a certain way — is a reliable source of misery. I think this is plainly true. Anyone who has loved someone and tried to control them knows it. Anyone who has gotten exactly what they wanted and felt the satisfaction drain away within weeks knows it. The hedonic treadmill is not a modern discovery; it is one of the oldest observations in human thought.

But here is where I part company with the more radical versions of the teaching. When the Buddhist ideal points toward the complete cessation of craving, or when the Stoic sage is described as someone who could watch his child die and remain undisturbed, I think we have crossed from wisdom into something that, for most of us, is either impossible or undesirable. Aristotle saw this clearly. He insisted that certain external goods — friendship, health, a measure of fortune — were not obstacles to the good life but constituents of it. To pretend otherwise struck him as a kind of philosophical vanity.

I think the honest position is somewhere in the middle, and I think most of the great thinkers, in their less systematic moments, knew this. Even the Buddha, after his years of extreme asceticism, chose what he called the Middle Way. Even Seneca, who wrote so eloquently about detachment, lived in considerable luxury and was not entirely a hypocrite for doing so — he argued that the point was not to refuse good things but to hold them lightly, ready to let them go.

That phrase — *hold them lightly* — is the closest I have come to a working principle. It means you can love deeply without demanding permanence. You can work toward something without staking your entire identity on the outcome. You can enjoy pleasure without believing it will last or that you cannot survive its absence. This is not indifference. It is, if anything, a more honest form of love, because it does not ask the world to be other than it is.

The difficulty, of course, is that we are not built for this. We are built to grasp. Attachment is not a philosophical error; it is a biological fact. The traditions that teach non-attachment are not describing something natural — they are describing a practice, something you work at for a lifetime and never fully achieve. I find that important to say, because the alternative is to read these teachings and feel like a failure for still wanting things. You are not failing. You are human. The point is not to stop wanting. The point is to see your wanting clearly — to notice the grip, and, where you can, to soften it.

Not to let go of everything. Just to hold it all a little more gently than you did yesterday.
