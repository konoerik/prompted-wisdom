You were not made for yourself alone. This was obvious to the ancients in a way it no longer is to us. Aristotle saw that we are political animals by nature, that a person outside society would have to be either a beast or a god. The Stoics went further. Marcus Aurelius, writing in his tent between battles, reminded himself each morning that he would encounter difficult people, but that he was made for cooperation the way hands and feet work together. To act against one another, he said, is to act against nature.

The Confucians built an entire ethical system on this foundation. For them, you become fully human only through your relationships—as child, parent, friend, citizen. There is no self that exists prior to these connections. You are not an isolated consciousness that happens to live among others. You are constituted by your bonds.

But here the tension begins, and it has never been resolved. Because while you are made for others, you can also be destroyed by them. Society gives you language, meaning, identity—and society also demands conformity, punishes difference, and enforces arrangements that serve some at the expense of others. The question is not whether to engage with society. You have no choice in that. The question is how to live within it without being consumed by it.

Epictetus, who had been a slave, understood this with particular clarity. He taught that you must distinguish between what is up to you and what is not. Other people's opinions, their approval, the roles society assigns you—these are not up to you. Your own judgments, your intentions, how you meet what comes—these are. This distinction is not about withdrawal. Epictetus expected his students to fulfill their duties as citizens, parents, friends. But he wanted them to do so freely, not as slaves to reputation or convention.

Montaigne, fifteen centuries later, found a different path to the same insight. He withdrew to his tower not to escape society but to know himself apart from it, to separate his own thoughts from the accumulated opinions he had inherited. He discovered that most of what he had taken as truth was merely custom. And custom, he saw, varies wildly. What is virtue in one place is vice in another. This did not make him a relativist. It made him skeptical of anyone who claimed absolute certainty about how life should be lived.

Nietzsche pushed this further and more dangerously. He saw that society's values often serve the interests of those who are already comfortable, that morality can be a tool of control. He asked you to question everything you have been taught about good and evil, to consider whether you have adopted values that work against your own flourishing. But Nietzsche was not advocating selfishness. He wanted you to become who you are, to create your own values—but this required tremendous discipline and honesty, not mere rebellion.

I have seen both errors in my years of walking through the world. I have met people so absorbed in society's demands that they have no idea who they are beneath the roles they play. And I have met people so committed to their own authenticity that they have become isolated, bitter, unable to cooperate or compromise. Neither path leads anywhere good.

What I have seen work is this: know the difference between your duties and your identity. Fulfill your obligations—to family, to community, to the stranger who needs help—but do not mistake these roles for your self. You are a citizen, a worker, perhaps a parent or partner, but you are not only these things. Tend to the part of you that exists apart from all social categories. Read, walk, sit quietly, think your own thoughts. Then return to your duties and fulfill them freely, as expressions of your own judgment rather than mere compliance.

Society will always ask too much of you. It will demand your conformity, your silence, your complicity in arrangements you know to be wrong. You cannot fix this. But you can decide, in each instance, what you will give and what you will withhold. This is not a formula. It is a practice, and it lasts your whole life.
