You will spend much of your twenties trying to become someone. This is natural. But beneath that effort lies a prior question that most people never ask directly: who are you already? Not who you want to be, not who your parents hoped you would become, not the person you present at job interviews or first dates — but the actual shape of your mind and temperament as it exists right now. The Stoics believed this question was the beginning of philosophy. I have come to think it is the beginning of a decent life.

Socrates walked through Athens asking people what they meant by the words they used so confidently — justice, courage, virtue — and discovered that almost no one could say. They had opinions, certainly, but opinions are not the same as understanding. He concluded that wisdom begins when you realize how little you know, especially about yourself. You think you know what you value until you are tested. You think you know what you want until you get it. Socrates was not being clever. He had watched people destroy themselves chasing things they did not actually desire, defending beliefs they had never examined, living according to scripts they had inherited without question. He thought the unexamined life was not worth living, but I think he meant something simpler: that an unexamined life is barely yours.

The Buddhists approached the same problem from a different direction. They sat still and watched their own minds, and what they found disturbed them. Thoughts arose without permission. Emotions surged and receded like weather. The self they assumed was solid and continuous turned out to be a process, not a thing — a river that never stopped moving. They concluded that most suffering comes from clinging to a fiction: the belief that you are a fixed, unchanging entity with a permanent nature. You are not. You are a collection of habits, memories, sensations, and reactions, many of which you did not choose and do not control. This sounds bleak until you realize it means you are not trapped. What arose through causes can change when the causes change.

Montaigne spent years in a tower writing essays — the word means attempts — trying to capture his own mind in motion. He wrote about his digestion, his fears, his contradictions, the gap between what he believed and what he did. He was not confessing or performing. He was investigating. He thought that every person contains the whole of human nature, and that if you look honestly at yourself, you will find everyone else there too. I have found this to be true. The things you are most ashamed of, the thoughts you believe make you uniquely broken — they are common. Montaigne was radical because he refused to pretend. He showed his work. He let the reader see him thinking, changing his mind, failing to live up to his own standards. In doing so, he made self-knowledge seem possible, even for ordinary people.

Nietzsche took this further. He believed most people never become themselves because they are too busy imitating others, adopting values handed to them by tradition, religion, or fashion. He urged his readers to create their own values, to become who they are rather than who they are told to be. This sounds like modern advice about authenticity, but Nietzsche meant something harder. Becoming yourself is not about discovering some inner essence that was always there, waiting to be revealed. It is about choosing, again and again, what you will affirm and what you will reject, until the pattern of those choices becomes a life. It requires honesty about what you actually love and fear, not what you think you should love and fear.

I have watched people spend decades avoiding this work. They are busy, they are successful, they are liked — and they are strangers to themselves. Then something breaks. An illness, a divorce, a failure that cannot be explained away. And in that break, the question returns: who am I when the performance stops? You do not need to wait for catastrophe. You can start now. Sit with yourself. Notice what you avoid noticing. Ask what you believe and why. The answers will change, and that is the point. Knowing yourself is not a destination. It is the work itself.
