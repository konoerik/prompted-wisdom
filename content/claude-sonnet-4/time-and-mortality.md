You will die, and you do not know when. This is the first fact, and everything else follows from how you hold it.

The Stoics said to keep death before your eyes daily. Marcus Aurelius wrote that you could leave life right now, and let that determine what you do and say and think. The Buddhist monks were more systematic about it: they meditated on corpses in various stages of decay, on the dissolution of the body, on the certainty that this flesh you carry will become food for worms. Both traditions understood that the contemplation of death is not morbid but clarifying. It strips away what is merely decorative in your concerns.

I have found this to be true, but only up to a point. The difficulty is that you cannot hold death steadily in view. The mind slides away from it. You read Marcus Aurelius in the morning and by afternoon you are annoyed that someone has taken your parking space. This is not failure. It is the condition. The awareness of mortality works not as a constant pressure but as a recurring correction, something you return to when you notice you have drifted into taking your days as infinite.

What the contemplation of death actually does, when it works, is restore proportion. The small humiliations shrink. The grudges you have been nursing reveal themselves as expensive hobbies. You see more clearly what you would regret leaving undone, and this is useful because regret, imagined forward, is one of the few reliable guides to what matters. Not the only guide—there are things worth doing that you would not regret leaving undone—but a reliable one.

The Epicureans took a different approach. Epicurus said that death is nothing to us, because when we exist, death is not present, and when death is present, we do not exist. Therefore death is not an evil. This is clever, and it has comforted many people, but I think it misses something. The dread of death is not primarily about the state of being dead. It is about the end of this particular stream of experience, this specific way of being in the world. You will not be here to see how things turn out. You will not finish what you started. The people you love will go on without you. That is the sting, and Epicurus's logic does not touch it.

What does touch it, I have found, is a kind of reconciliation with incompleteness. You will not finish. No one finishes. The work continues after you, or it does not, but either way your part in it is temporary. The Confucians understood this better than the Greeks. They spoke of planting trees under whose shade you would never sit, of beginning projects that would take generations to complete. There is a dignity in this, in accepting that you are one link in a chain, that your contribution is real but partial.

The other thing that helps is to notice that you are always living in the present moment, and that this has always been true, and will be true until it is not. Seneca said we are not given a short life but we make it short, and we are not ill-supplied but wasteful of it. He meant that most of what we call living is a kind of absence, a distraction, a failure to be where we are. I think he was right. The problem is not that life is short but that presence is difficult.

You cannot be present by trying to be present. That is like trying to fall asleep by trying to fall asleep. But you can notice when you are absent, when you are living in the rehearsal of future conversations or the rehashing of past ones, and you can return. Again and again, you can return. This is not a solution to mortality. It is a way of not wasting the time you have on the pretense that you have more of it than you do.

The traditions converge here: death is certain, the hour is uncertain, and this should change how you live. Not by making you frantic, but by making you serious. By returning you to what is in front of you.
