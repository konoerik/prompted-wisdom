You will die, and you do not know when. This is not meant to frighten you. It is meant to wake you up.

The Stoics understood this with a clarity that can still startle. Seneca wrote to a friend that we act as if we will live forever, postponing what matters, spending our days as though they cost us nothing. But time, he said, is the one thing we can actually lose. You cannot store it. You cannot earn it back. Every moment you waste belongs to death already. Marcus Aurelius, writing in his tent between campaigns, reminded himself each morning that he might not see evening. Not as morbid ritual, but as practice—a way of asking what deserved his attention if this day were all he had.

The Buddhists arrived at something similar from a different direction. Impermanence is not a problem to solve but a truth to accept. Everything arises and passes. Your body, your relationships, your sense of who you are—all of it is in motion, already dissolving even as it forms. To cling to permanence is to suffer. But to see clearly that nothing lasts is also to see that this moment, right now, is all you ever have. Not in some vague inspirational sense. Literally. The past is gone. The future is imagined. Only now is real.

I have sat with people who were dying, and I have noticed that very few of them wished they had spent more time worrying. What they mourned was presence—moments they were too distracted to inhabit, people they loved but never quite showed up for. They did not regret failing to optimize their lives. They regretted not living them.

Heidegger, difficult as he is, saw that we spend most of our time fleeing from the fact of death. We lose ourselves in busyness, in chatter, in the endless trivial concerns that let us forget we are finite. He called this inauthenticity—not as moral judgment, but as description. To live authentically, he argued, is to face your mortality squarely, to let it clarify what matters. Being-toward-death, he called it. Only when you accept that your time is limited do you stop drifting and start choosing.

Montaigne spent years thinking about death, reading the Stoics, preparing himself. Then he fell from a horse and nearly died, and in that moment all his preparation vanished. What remained was not philosophy but sensation—the strange lightness of letting go. Afterward, he wrote that to practice philosophy is to learn how to die, but he meant something gentler than the Stoics did. Not discipline and control, but familiarity. To think about death often enough that it loses its terror. To let it sit beside you, a quiet companion that makes the colors brighter.

I have walked in places where death is more visible than it is in the insulated corners of the modern world. In a village in Nepal after an earthquake. In a hospital in a city I will not name. What I noticed was not despair but a kind of tenderness. People were kinder to each other. They wasted less time on pretense. Mortality, when it is close, has a way of burning off what does not matter.

Nietzsche said we should live as though we would have to live this exact life again, eternally. Every choice, every moment, repeated forever. Would you choose it? Not as test of perfection, but of honesty. Are you living in a way you can affirm, or are you waiting for permission to begin?

Viktor Frankl, who survived the camps, wrote that everything can be taken from a person except the freedom to choose their attitude. Even facing death, especially facing death, you have this. Not control over what happens, but choice in how you meet it.

The thread that runs through all of this is simple: mortality is not the enemy of meaning. It is the condition of it. Because your time is finite, your choices matter. Because this moment will not come again, it deserves your full attention. You do not need to achieve anything grand. You need to be here, awake, while you still can.
