You will spend roughly a third of your waking life working. This is not a small thing. And yet most of what we inherit about work is confused — tangled up with status, with proving ourselves, with the desperate hope that the right career will finally make us feel complete.

I have met people who loved their work and people who endured it. I have met those who found purpose in what they did and those who found it elsewhere. What I have not met, in any lasting sense, are people who felt fulfilled by work that had no connection to anything beyond their own advancement. The Stoics understood this. Epictetus, who had been a slave, said that freedom was not about choosing your circumstances but about choosing your response to them. He did not say work must be grand. He said it must be done well, with attention, as part of a larger whole. Marcus Aurelius, an emperor with every privilege, wrote in his private notes that even the smallest task could be meaningful if it served the common good. He was trying to remind himself of this. The reminder was necessary because the temptation to see work as beneath us, or as merely instrumental, is constant.

The Confucians took this further. They believed that all roles — parent, teacher, craftsman, ruler — carried inherent dignity, but only if performed with sincerity and skill. Work was not separate from character. It was where character showed itself. Confucius said that the superior person is not a tool, meaning that we should not reduce ourselves to mere function, but he also believed that function mattered. To do your work poorly, to phone it in, was to diminish yourself. This was not about productivity. It was about integrity.

The Buddhists approached it differently but arrived at something similar. Right livelihood, one of the steps of the Eightfold Path, meant work that did not cause harm. But it also meant work done with mindfulness, without the constant grasping for results. I once spoke with a woman in northern Thailand who made pottery. She said her teacher had told her that if she worked only for the finished pot, she would miss the clay. The work itself was the point. Not as drudgery, but as presence. Thich Nhat Hanh later wrote that washing dishes could be as sacred as meditation if you were fully there while doing it. This sounds precious until you try it. Then you realize how rarely you are fully anywhere.

Montaigne, centuries later, was skeptical of grand purpose. He thought most of us were fooling ourselves, dressing up ambition as calling. He preferred the modest satisfactions of a life well-tended — his estate, his essays, his friendships. He was not lazy. He worked constantly. But he refused to pretend that his work was more important than it was. There is something clarifying in this. Not every job is a vocation. Some work is just work. And that is fine, as long as you do not let it hollow you out.

Nietzsche disagreed. He believed that without a sense of purpose, without something to strive toward, we would collapse into nihilism. He was right about the danger, but perhaps too harsh about the remedy. Not everyone needs to create new values or become who they are in some dramatic sense. Some people find purpose in raising children well, in showing up for their community, in doing ordinary work with care. Frankl, who survived the concentration camps, saw this more clearly. He wrote that meaning could be found in three ways: through creating something, through loving someone, or through the attitude we take toward unavoidable suffering. Work could provide the first, but it was not the only path. And when work itself became suffering, meaning could still be found in how we bore it.

I think the thread that runs through all of this is simple: work matters not because it defines you, but because how you work reveals you. You do not need to love your job. You do not need to find your passion. But you do need to find some way to show up honestly, to contribute something, to not waste the hours. Because the hours are your life, and you do not get them back.
