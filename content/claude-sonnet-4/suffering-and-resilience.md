Suffering is not a problem to be solved but a condition to be understood. I came to this slowly, because everything in our moment pushes against it. We are told that pain is always provisional, that the right technique or insight or purchase will deliver us from it. But the older texts do not promise this. They begin elsewhere: with the plain fact that to live is to encounter loss, frustration, illness, and death. The question is not whether these things will come, but how we meet them when they do.

The Buddha made this his first teaching. Life is dukkha—a word often translated as suffering, though it means something closer to unsatisfactoriness, the way even pleasant things carry within them the seed of their ending. He was not being pessimistic. He was clearing the ground. If you start by expecting life to be otherwise, you will spend your days in argument with reality. The path he offered was not escape but a kind of clear-eyed attention: see craving for what it is, see how it binds you, and in that seeing find a measure of freedom.

The Stoics arrived at something similar from a different direction. Epictetus said that we suffer not from events themselves but from our judgments about them. A thing happens—a betrayal, an illness, a failure—and we add to it a story about how it should not have happened, how it ruins everything, how we cannot bear it. The event is one thing; the story is another. This does not mean pretending the event does not hurt. It means recognizing where the hurt ends and where our elaboration of it begins. Marcus Aurelius, writing to himself in the middle of a plague and a war, returned to this again and again: you have power over your mind, not outside events. Realize this, and you will find strength.

I have found this true, but only up to a point. There is a coldness in the Stoic position if taken too far, a suggestion that we can think our way free of grief or despair. We cannot. Some losses are not stories we tell ourselves; they are ruptures in the fabric of a life. The death of a child, the betrayal of trust, the slow unraveling of the self in illness—these are not problems of judgment. They are encounters with the limits of what we can control, and no amount of reframing will make them otherwise.

What the Stoics do offer, though, is a way to distinguish between the wound and the infection. The wound is what happens. The infection is what we do to ourselves afterward: the bitterness we cultivate, the resentment we feed, the refusal to let the world be anything other than the site of our injury. This is where resilience lives—not in avoiding pain, but in not letting it colonize everything.

Resilience, I think, is less about strength than about flexibility. The image that comes to me is from the Tao Te Ching: the tree that bends in the wind survives the storm, while the rigid one breaks. This is not passivity. It is a kind of active yielding, a willingness to let circumstances move through you without insisting they conform to your will. Lao Tzu says the softest things in the world overcome the hardest. Water wears away stone not by force but by persistence and adaptation.

But resilience also requires something the Taoists do not emphasize enough: connection. We do not endure suffering well in isolation. The Stoic ideal of self-sufficiency has its place, but it can become a trap, a way of armoring ourselves against the very relationships that make life bearable. I have seen people survive terrible things not because they were strong in themselves, but because they allowed themselves to be held by others. This is not weakness. It is a different kind of strength.

What holds up, then, is this: suffering is woven into the structure of life. We do not overcome it by denying it or by pretending we are above it. We meet it by seeing it clearly, by not adding unnecessary weight to what is already heavy, and by remaining open—to change, to help, to the possibility that even in the midst of pain, something can still move.
