You will suffer. Not as punishment, not because you deserve it, but because you are alive. The question is not whether suffering will find you, but what you will make of it when it does.

I have sat with people in the aftermath of loss — a child gone, a marriage ended, a body that no longer works as it once did — and I have noticed something. The ones who endure are not the ones who avoid pain or pretend it isn't there. They are the ones who stop asking why it happened and start asking what they will do now that it has.

The Stoics understood this. Epictetus, who spent years as a slave before gaining his freedom, taught that we do not control what happens to us, only how we respond. He divided the world into two categories: things within our power and things beyond it. Most of what we suffer over — the past, other people's choices, the fact of mortality itself — belongs to the second category. We exhaust ourselves trying to change what cannot be changed. The Stoics said: stop. Turn your attention to what remains in your hands. Your judgments. Your actions. Your character.

This is not resignation. It is the opposite. It is the refusal to waste your life fighting battles that cannot be won so that you have strength left for the ones that can.

Marcus Aurelius, writing to himself in the middle of a plague and a war, reminded himself each morning that he would encounter difficult people, that plans would fail, that the day would not go as he hoped. He did this not to be pessimistic but to be prepared. He called it premeditation. When suffering arrived, it would not catch him off guard. He would meet it as something expected, something he had already made room for.

The Buddhists came at the same truth from a different direction. The First Noble Truth is that life contains suffering — dukkha, a word that means not just pain but the fundamental unsatisfactoriness of existence, the way even good things pass and leave us wanting. But the Buddhists did not stop there. They said suffering has a cause: our clinging, our insistence that things be other than they are. And because it has a cause, it has a remedy. We can learn to hold life more lightly. Not to care less, but to love without grasping, to be present without demanding permanence.

I have seen this in practice. A woman I met in Nepal who lost her home in an earthquake told me she grieved not for the house but for the photographs inside it. Then she said: "But I still have the memories. Those were never in the house." She had found a way to hold what mattered without needing the container.

Nietzsche took the question further. He asked: what if suffering is not something to be merely endured but something that can make you stronger? He wrote that what does not kill you makes you stronger, though the phrase has been so overused it has lost its edge. What he meant was this: suffering can destroy you, or it can become the forge in which you are remade. The choice is yours, but only if you stop seeing yourself as a victim and start seeing yourself as someone capable of transformation.

Viktor Frankl, who survived Auschwitz, built on this. He said that suffering ceases to be suffering the moment it finds meaning. In the camps, he noticed that those who survived were not necessarily the strongest or the healthiest, but those who had something to live for — a task unfinished, a person waiting, a story that needed telling. Meaning, he said, is the difference between endurance and collapse.

I do not know if suffering makes you stronger. I have seen it break people beyond repair. But I have also seen people carry unbearable weight and somehow keep walking. What they had in common was this: they did not ask the universe to be fair. They did not wait for the pain to make sense. They found something — one small thing — that mattered more than their suffering, and they moved toward it.

That is resilience. Not the absence of pain, but the presence of purpose.
