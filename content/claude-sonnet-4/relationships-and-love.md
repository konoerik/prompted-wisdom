You cannot love another person well if you are at war with yourself. This is not a romantic idea. It is a practical one, and it appears in nearly every tradition that has thought seriously about human connection. The Stoics said it plainly: if you are enslaved to your own passions, you will try to enslave others to them as well. The Buddhists put it differently but meant something similar: clinging arises from a self that feels incomplete, and clinging is the opposite of love. Confucius, always concerned with the social fabric, insisted that one must first cultivate oneself before attempting to order one's household. The sequence matters. You do not skip steps.

This does not mean you must perfect yourself before you can love. That would be another kind of tyranny. It means you must at least know where you end and the other person begins. You must be able to sit with your own loneliness, your own fear, your own need, without immediately converting those feelings into demands. Most of what we call love is actually this conversion process. We feel empty, so we ask another person to fill us. We feel uncertain, so we ask them to prove themselves. We feel afraid of being left, so we tighten our grip. None of this is love. It is commerce.

Real love, the kind that lasts and deepens, requires what the Greeks called *phronesis*—practical wisdom. It is not enough to feel strongly. Feelings are weather. They come and go, and if you build your life on them alone, you will be perpetually rebuilding. What matters is what you do when the feeling fades, when the other person is difficult, when you are tired and they are needy, or when you are needy and they are tired. This is where virtue enters. The Stoics were right about this: love is not primarily an emotion but a practice, a set of actions repeated until they become character.

But the Stoics, for all their wisdom, sometimes made love sound like duty, and duty alone can become cold. The Buddhists offer a corrective here with their notion of *metta*, often translated as loving-kindness. It is a warmth that extends outward without grasping, a genuine wish for another's well-being that does not depend on what they give you in return. This is closer to what love feels like when it is healthy. You want good things for the other person not because it serves you, but because their flourishing has become, in some mysterious way, part of your own.

Still, we must be careful. There is a version of selflessness that is actually self-erasure, and it helps no one. I have seen people disappear into relationships, and what they call love is often fear wearing a disguise. Aristotle was wise to distinguish between different kinds of friendship. Some are based on utility, some on pleasure, and some on mutual recognition of virtue. Only the last kind endures. Only the last kind allows both people to remain whole.

You will know you are loving well when you can hold two things at once: a deep commitment to the other person's reality, and a deep commitment to your own. This is harder than it sounds. It means you do not lie to keep the peace. It means you do not betray yourself to be loved. It means you allow conflict, because conflict is often just two truths trying to coexist. The goal is not to avoid difficulty but to meet it without cruelty, without contempt, without the small murders of daily resentment.

In the end, love is attention. Simone Weil said this, and I have found it to be true. Not the attention of surveillance, but the attention of genuine interest. You look at the other person and see them, not as a reflection of your needs, not as a character in your story, but as a separate consciousness, as mysterious and vast as your own. This kind of seeing is rare. It requires you to quiet yourself. But when it happens, even briefly, it is the closest thing to grace that most of us will know.
