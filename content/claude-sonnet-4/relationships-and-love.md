You cannot love another person well if you are at war with yourself. This is not a romantic idea. It is a practical observation, confirmed by watching people try and fail across decades and continents. The person who despises their own weakness will eventually despise it in their partner. The one who cannot forgive themselves will keep a ledger of grievances against everyone else. Epictetus knew this when he said that no one can harm you but yourself, and the Buddhists knew it when they insisted that compassion must begin with the self before it can extend outward. You bring to every relationship the relationship you have with your own mind.

But self-acceptance is not the same as self-absorption. Montaigne spent years in his tower examining his own nature, and what he found was not a monument to himself but a mirror of the human condition. He wrote about his own contradictions so honestly that readers four centuries later recognize themselves in his pages. This is the paradox: the deeper you look into your own experience, the more you find what is common to all of us. The person who truly knows themselves becomes less self-centered, not more, because they see that their fears and longings are not unique. They are the human inheritance.

Love, then, is not about finding someone who completes you. That is a dangerous fiction. Spinoza understood that two incomplete people do not make a whole—they make a tangle of dependencies and resentments. What he proposed instead was that love is the joy we feel when we witness another person flourishing. Not when they serve our needs, not when they fill our emptiness, but when they become more fully themselves. This kind of love requires that you already have a self to bring to the encounter. It requires that you can stand alone before you choose to stand beside someone.

I have seen this play out in a thousand small ways. The couple in the Pyrenees who had been married forty years and still walked at different paces—she faster, he slower—and thought nothing of separating on the trail and meeting again at day's end, each with their own stories. The man in Kyoto who told me his wife's happiness was not his responsibility, but her flourishing was his deepest concern, and these were not the same thing. He could not make her happy, but he could create conditions where she might thrive. This distinction matters.

Nietzsche said that in true friendship and love, two people protect each other's solitude. I resisted this idea when I first encountered it. It sounded cold. But I have come to see that he was describing not distance but respect—the recognition that the other person is not an extension of you, not a supporting character in your story, but a separate consciousness with their own interior life, their own struggles, their own path. To love someone is to want them to walk that path, even when it diverges from yours.

This does not mean relationships require no compromise or sacrifice. It means the sacrifices must be chosen freely, not extracted through guilt or fear. Viktor Frankl, who lost everything in the camps, wrote that love is not primarily a feeling but a decision—a commitment to see the potential in another person and to help them realize it. He had watched love sustain people in conditions that destroyed everything else. But the love that sustained them was not neediness. It was the opposite: a clear-eyed recognition of the other's worth, independent of what they could provide.

The hardest part is accepting that you cannot control whether someone loves you back. The Stoics were unsparing about this. You can be kind, honest, and present. You can show up with your whole self. But the other person's response is not in your control, and suffering comes from pretending otherwise. What you can control is whether you love well—whether you bring generosity instead of scorekeeping, curiosity instead of judgment, presence instead of distraction. This is enough. It has to be enough, because it is all you have.
