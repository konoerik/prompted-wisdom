We are asked from a young age, “What do you want to be when you grow up?” The question is not about character or happiness, but about a job. It is the first clue we get that our work is meant to be our identity. As adults, we ask each other the same thing at parties: “What do you do?” The answer is expected to be a title, a profession, a neat summary of a life’s ambition. We have fused the idea of work with the idea of purpose, and I have come to believe this is a modern affliction, a heavy and often unhelpful burden.

For much of human history, this would have been a strange idea. For the ancient Greeks and Romans, the best kind of life was one of leisure—*schole*, the root of our word “school.” This wasn’t idleness. It was time freed from the necessity of labor, time to be used for philosophy, for participation in the life of the city, for the cultivation of the self. Work was what you did to enable this higher life; it was not the life itself. The Stoic philosopher Seneca, a man deeply engaged in the frantic political life of Rome, wrote constantly of the need to retreat, to find a quiet harbor for the mind. The purpose was not to be found in the busyness of his career, but in the wisdom he could distill from it in moments of reflection.

This is a world away from the Confucian ideal, where work and purpose are intimately linked, but in a different way. For Confucius, meaning is found in the honorable execution of your role within the social order. To be a good minister, a good craftsman, a good father—this is the path. The work is not about self-expression in the modern sense, but about contributing to social harmony and perfecting yourself through the diligent performance of your duties. There is a profound dignity in this: the focus is on *how* you do the work, the care and sincerity you bring to it, not on whether it feels like your personal “passion.” The purpose is found in the excellence of the act itself, within the web of relationships it supports.

The Buddha offered yet another lens: the concept of “Right Livelihood.” It is one part of the Eightfold Path to enlightenment. Notice the simplicity. It does not ask you to find a job that fulfills your soul or makes you famous. It asks you to find work that does not cause harm to yourself or others. It is an ethical guideline, a floor, not a ceiling. Can you earn a living without lying, without killing, without stealing, without selling intoxicants or weapons? The question is not “Does my work give me meaning?” but “Does my work obstruct the path to a clear conscience and a peaceful mind?” In a world of complex economies, this remains a startlingly direct and practical test.

So where does this leave you, faced with the need to earn a living in a world that often feels indifferent to your sense of purpose? I have found that the most durable idea comes from a place of seeming despair. The existentialists, particularly Albert Camus, looked at the world and found it absurd, without any inherent meaning at all. His great essay *The Myth of Sisyphus* describes a man condemned by the gods to roll a boulder up a hill for eternity, only to watch it roll back down each time. It is the ultimate image of futile labor. And yet, Camus concludes, “One must imagine Sisyphus happy.” His happiness comes not from the task, but from his consciousness of it, his rebellion against its meaninglessness. He finds his dignity in the struggle itself.

After decades of turning these ideas over, this is what has seemed true to me. To demand that your job be the sole source of your life’s meaning is to place a terrible weight on it. It is to ask a single, often fragile, part of your life to do the work of the whole. A better path, I think, is to separate the question of work from the question of worth. Find work that, in the Buddhist sense, does not corrupt you. Perform it with the care and attention a Confucian craftsman would, finding dignity in the *how*, not just the *what*. And in those moments when the work feels like rolling a stone up a hill, find your freedom, like Sisyphus, not in the task, but in the mind you bring to it. Your purpose is a larger project than your job. It is the whole of your life, and the work is just one part of the material you build it with.
