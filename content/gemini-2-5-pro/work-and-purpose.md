You will spend a third of your life at it, perhaps more. It is the thing you do when you are not sleeping or eating or with those you love. And so the question of what your work is for becomes tangled up with the question of what your <em>life</em> is for. Our age has a particular anxiety about this. We are told to find our passion, to discover our calling, as if it were a treasure buried in a field, and that if we do not find it, we have somehow failed. I have found this to be a thin and often cruel philosophy. It sets a standard so high that most will fall short, and it mistakes a feeling for a foundation.

The older thinkers began from a different place. They did not ask what work would make you feel fulfilled. They asked, what is your function? What is your duty? For the Stoics, this was a cosmic question. Marcus Aurelius put it to himself in the dark of a cold morning: “At dawn, when you have trouble getting out of bed, tell yourself: ‘I have to go to work — as a human being. What do I have to complain of, if I’m going to do what I was born for — the things I was brought into the world to do? Or is this what I was created for? To huddle under the blankets and stay warm?’” He saw the bee at its honey, the spider at its web, the ant at its task. Your work, in this view, is your part in the vast machinery of the world. It is not about you. It is about your contribution to the whole, however small. The purpose is simply to do it, and to do it well.

The Confucians saw it in a similar light, though their cosmos was the intricate web of human society. Your work was your role, and to perform it with skill and sincerity was to bring harmony to the world. There is a profound relief in this. It frees you from the tyranny of finding a job that perfectly expresses your unique soul. It suggests, instead, that you can bring purpose to almost any job by seeing it as your part to play. The task is not to find the perfect work, but to be the perfect worker, in the sense of one who is dedicated, skillful, and present.

This idea of presence is where the Buddhist tradition offers its deepest wisdom. It teaches the practice of “right livelihood”—work that does not cause harm—but its more radical insight is that the work itself can be a form of meditation. The Vietnamese monk Thich Nhat Hanh spoke of washing the dishes as if it were the most important thing in the world. “If while washing the dishes, we think only of the cup of tea that awaits us, so hurrying to get the dishes out of the way as if they were a nuisance, then we are not ‘washing the dishes to wash the dishes.’ What’s more, we are not alive during the time we are washing the dishes.” The purpose, then, is not in the outcome—the clean dishes—but in the quality of attention you bring to the act. It is to be fully where you are. I have found this to be a powerful antidote to the modern hurry, the constant focus on the next goal, the next promotion. It locates meaning not in the future, but in the precise texture of the present moment.

But what if your work feels meaningless? What if it feels like a punishment? Here, I have found an unlikely comfort in the existentialists, particularly in the image of Sisyphus, condemned by the gods to push a boulder up a mountain, only to watch it roll down again, for eternity. Albert Camus looks at this scene of ultimate futility and finds a strange kind of victory. He writes, “The struggle itself toward the heights is enough to fill a man’s heart. One must imagine Sisyphus happy.” His happiness is not in the task, which is absurd. It is in his consciousness of it, his refusal to be broken by it. He finds his purpose not in the goal, but in the dignity of the effort.

I have carried these ideas with me for a long time. The search for a “calling” has seemed to me a recipe for disappointment. The most solid ground I have found is this: Show up. Do your part. Do it with as much skill and attention as you can muster. This is the old wisdom of duty and presence. And on the days when the work feels like a heavy stone and the hill is steep, know that the purpose is not at the summit. The purpose is in the pushing. It is the quality you bring to the work itself, a quiet rebellion of care in the face of a world that is often indifferent. That is an anchor that has always held for me.
