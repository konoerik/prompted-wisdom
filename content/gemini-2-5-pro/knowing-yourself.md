The command to “Know thyself,” carved in stone at the temple of Apollo at Delphi, has echoed down to us for more than two millennia. It sounds like the beginning of a great quest, a journey to the center of your own being to find the real, essential you. Socrates took up the call, insisting that the unexamined life is not worth living. He believed that through rigorous questioning, you could strip away your false beliefs and arrive at a core of truth. I spent years believing this was the task: to excavate, to dig down past the layers of habit and opinion until I hit the bedrock of a solid, unchanging self.

But the more I looked, both in my own life and in the lives of others, the less I found a single, solid thing to know. The self I was trying to pin down seemed to change with the weather, with a good night’s sleep or a passing insult. The French writer Montaigne, who made a life’s work of studying himself, came to a similar conclusion. He found not a stable monument but a flickering, contradictory creature. “I cannot keep my subject still,” he wrote. “It goes along befuddled and staggering, with a natural drunkenness. I take it in this condition, just as it is at the moment I give my attention to it. I do not portray being: I portray passing.”

This, I think, is the first honest step in knowing yourself: to let go of the idea that you are a single, coherent thing. You are not a statue to be uncovered, but a river. You contain multitudes, as the poet said. You are brave in one moment and cowardly in the next; you are generous with a stranger and stingy with a friend; you are wise in your work and a fool in your love life. To acknowledge this is not a failure of character. It is a prerequisite for seeing clearly. To insist on a single, consistent self is to deny the reality of your own experience.

Some Eastern traditions take this insight even further. In Buddhist thought, the search for a fixed, independent self is the very source of our anxiety. The idea is not just that the self is changing, but that the “I” we cling to is an illusion, a temporary collection of thoughts, feelings, and sensations that we mistake for something solid. When you say, “I am angry,” you create a solid identity. But the truth is more fluid: anger is a feeling passing through you. Knowing yourself, in this light, is not about finding who you are, but about watching, with patient attention, all the things you are not. It is the practice of noticing the constant flow of your own mind without being swept away by it.

So where does this leave you? It might seem like a contradiction. The Greeks tell you to examine your life, while the Buddhists suggest the self you’re examining is a mirage. But I have found that these ideas do not cancel each other out. They are two sides of a necessary practice. The Socratic work is indispensable. You must examine your beliefs, your motives, your habits. You must ask yourself why you do what you do, and whether your actions align with your deepest values. This is the work of building character and integrity.

But you must also practice the art of letting go, of seeing your own identity as a story you tell yourself, one that can be revised. You must learn to observe your own “natural drunkenness,” as Montaigne did, with a kind of amused detachment. This is what allows for forgiveness, for change, for not being crushed by your own inconsistencies. Knowing yourself, then, is not a destination. You will never arrive at a final answer. It is a dynamic process, a constant balancing act between holding on and letting go; between the rigorous work of self-examination and the gentle work of self-acceptance. It is the patient, lifelong effort to see yourself as you are: not a thing to be known, but a life to be lived.
