Of all the truths the old books offer, none is so stark or so difficult to hold in the mind as the simple fact of your own ending. We know it, of course, but we live as if we do not. We defer, we delay, we act as though our supply of days is infinite. The first and most practical lesson I ever learned about time came from the Romans, particularly Seneca, who put it with a clarity that cuts through two thousand years of noise. It is not that we have a short time to live, he said, but that we waste a great deal of it. Life is long enough, and a sufficiently generous amount has been given to us for the highest achievements if it were all well invested.

This is not a morbid thought. It is an invigorating one. The knowledge of death is not meant to cast a shadow over life, but to illuminate it. It is the sharp awareness of the frame that gives value to the picture. The Stoics called this practice *memento mori*—remember you must die. I have found this is not a call to fear, but a call to attention. When you remember that your time is finite, you are less likely to squander it on trivialities, on nursing grudges, on chasing applause. You begin to see each day not as one of an endless series, but as a discrete and precious gift. Marcus Aurelius, writing in his tent on the northern frontier, would remind himself of the vast abyss of time behind him and the infinity of time to come. In that context, his own life was a mere blip. This perspective did not make his life feel meaningless; it made the present moment, the only thing he truly possessed, infinitely meaningful.

But what of the fear itself? The cold dread of non-existence? For this, I have found no better antidote than the one offered by Epicurus. His logic is so simple it feels almost like a trick, yet it holds. “Death,” he wrote, “is nothing to us. For what has been dissolved has no sensation, and what has no sensation is nothing to us.” When you exist, death does not. When death exists, you do not. Why, then, he asks, should you fear a state you will never experience? You do not lament the time before you were born; why should you lament the time after you are gone? I have carried these lines with me for decades, and while they may not erase the instinctive animal fear of dying, they disarm the philosophical terror of being dead. They are a rational anchor in an emotional storm.

A similar thought, though it takes a different shape, surfaces in the East. The Buddhist teaching on impermanence is not just that you will die, but that you are, in a sense, dying and being reborn in every moment. Everything is in flux. The person you were a decade ago is gone; the person you are now will be gone in a moment. There is no solid, unchanging “you” to be extinguished in the first place. To cling to life is to cling to a wave in the ocean, a source of inevitable suffering. The practice is not to fear the wave’s passing, but to see the beauty of its brief and unrepeatable form. This is a harder path, I think, for a Western mind to walk, but its goal is the same: to liberate you from the prison of a future that will never arrive and a past that is already gone.

Over the years, I have found that these ideas do not contradict one another. They are tools for different moments. The Stoic’s urgency is for when you are wasting your life. The Epicurean’s logic is for when you are afraid of the dark. The Buddhist’s acceptance is for when you are clinging too tightly to what is already slipping away. They all converge on a single point: this moment. The great gift of mortality is that it makes life matter. It is the final deadline that compels you to do the work, to say the words, to forgive the slight, to notice the light on the leaves. To live as if you will live forever is to live as if nothing matters. To know that your time is short is to know that everything does.
