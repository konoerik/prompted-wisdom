Of all the facts of life, the one that presses hardest is the one we most wish to ignore: the clock is ticking. From the moment you are old enough to understand it, you know your time is finite. For many, this knowledge is a source of low-grade, persistent anxiety. For others, it is a terror that must be kept at bay with noise and distraction. The hours and years seem to slip through our fingers like sand, and we are left grasping at nothing. I have found that the oldest thinkers have no magic to stop this flow, but they offer something better: a way to stand within it, clear-eyed and unafraid.

The first and most bracing lesson comes from the Stoics. Seneca, in his letter *On the Shortness of Life*, makes a point that has stayed with me for decades. He argues that our problem is not that we have too little time, but that we waste so much of it. “It is not that we have a short time to live,” he wrote, “but that we waste a great deal of it.” We give our days away to pointless obligations, to anxieties about a future that may never arrive, to nostalgia for a past we cannot change. We live as if we are immortal, only to be shocked when the end draws near. His remedy is simple and severe: treat your time as your most precious possession. Guard it jealously. Ask of any activity, any person, any worry: is this worthy of a piece of my life?

His fellow Stoic, the Roman emperor Marcus Aurelius, saw time as a river of passing events, a rushing torrent. He reminded himself constantly that the past is gone and the future is not yet here. The only thing you truly possess is the present moment. “Even if you were destined to live three thousand years, or ten times that,” he wrote in his private journal, “remember that no one loses any other life than the one he is living, and no one lives any other life than the one he is losing.” This is not a call to hedonism, but a call to attention. This moment, right now, is the only field of action you will ever have. To be fully present in it is to live the only life available to you.

This focus on the present finds a powerful echo in the Buddhist tradition. The core teaching of *anicca*, or impermanence, is not a morbid obsession with decay. It is a simple statement of fact: everything is in a state of flux. Your thoughts, your body, the people you love, the world itself—it is all changing, always. The suffering, the Buddhists say, comes from trying to cling to things as if they were permanent. By accepting that everything, including yourself, is a temporary arrangement, you can let go of the desperate need to hold on. You learn to appreciate the beauty of the fleeting moment without being shattered when it passes.

Of course, it is one thing to accept the passing of a moment, and another to accept your own final passing. On this, the ancient Greek philosopher Epicurus offered a kind of logical anesthetic. “Death is nothing to us,” he argued, “for that which has been dissolved lacks sensation; and that which lacks sensation is nothing to us.” When you exist, death does not; when death exists, you do not. Why, he asks, should you be terrified of a state you will never experience? For many, this feels too cold, a philosopher’s trick. But I have found it to be a useful tool for the mind, a way to dismantle the *fear* of death, which is often more painful than the thought of death itself.

Perhaps the most humane guide I have found is the French essayist Montaigne, who took these older ideas and made them his own. He believed that “to philosophize is to learn how to die.” He did not mean this in a grim way. He meant that by thinking about death, by familiarizing himself with it, he could rob it of its power to terrorize him. He practiced imagining his own end, not to be morbid, but to be prepared. By making death a familiar thought, he found he could live more freely, less burdened by the dread of it. He was no longer a hostage to the unknown.

After all these years of reading, I have not found a single idea that promises to make you immortal or stop time. What I have found is a convergence on a single, difficult truth. The awareness of your mortality is not a curse. It is the frame that gives your life its shape and meaning. It is the thing that gives urgency to your love, purpose to your work, and sweetness to your simple, passing pleasures. You are here for a short while. The great thinkers do not offer a way out of that. They simply, and profoundly, ask you to be here for it. The frame doesn’t diminish the painting; it’s what makes it possible to see it at all.
