Of all the subjects the great thinkers turned their minds to, love is the one where their words can feel most distant from the heat and confusion of our own hearts. They speak of abstract forms and universal duties, while you are left navigating the dizzying reality of a single, irreplaceable person. And yet, I have found that if you listen patiently, their voices can offer a clarity that is hard to find elsewhere. They can help you distinguish the solid ground from the marsh.

I have found no better starting point than Aristotle’s simple division of friendship. He saw that we connect with others for three reasons: for utility, for pleasure, or for the good. Friendships of utility are the most common; you and a colleague help each other at work, or you and a neighbor watch each other’s houses. They are built on mutual benefit and last only as long as the benefit does. Friendships of pleasure are also familiar, especially in youth. You share a hobby, a sense of humor, a love of the same music. These are warmer, but they too fade when the shared pleasure wanes. You will recognize both of these in your own life, and there is no shame in them. They are part of the fabric of a human life. But they are not the thing itself.

The third kind, which Aristotle called a friendship of virtue, is the one that matters. Here, you love the other person not for what they can do for you or how they make you feel, but for who they are. You wish them well, as he put it, “for their own sake.” This is a bond between two people who admire and respect the character of the other, and who, in each other’s company, are helped to become better versions of themselves. These friendships are rare because good character is rare, and they take a long time to form. They are the relationships that can withstand distance and disagreement, because they are not built on the shifting sands of convenience or amusement, but on the bedrock of who you both are.

Centuries later, the French essayist Montaigne wrote of his profound friendship with Étienne de La Boétie, a bond so complete it seemed to defy even Aristotle’s neat categories. When pressed to explain it, he could only say, “Because it was he, because it was I.” In that simple phrase, he captures the mystery at the heart of true connection. It is a recognition, a meeting of souls that feels both freely chosen and utterly fated. If you are lucky enough to experience this even once, you will know that it is the greatest of human goods.

But what of loss? The Stoics and the Buddhists, from their different corners of the world, looked this question squarely in the eye. They saw that the deepest love brings with it the potential for the deepest pain. Their answer was not to love less, but to love with wisdom. They remind you that everything is impermanent. The people you love are mortal. Your relationship, in its current form, will one day end. The Stoic philosopher Epictetus gave this stark advice: “When you kiss your child, or your wife, say that you are kissing a mortal. Then you will not be so distraught if they die.” This may sound cold to you, but I have come to see it as a profound act of love. It is a call to be present, to cherish the person beside you <em>now</em>, without clinging, without the illusion that you possess them. It is an attempt to love the person, not your idea of a future with them.

Ultimately, what has seemed most true to me is that the quality of your relationships is not determined by what you find in others, but by what you cultivate in yourself. The Confucians spoke of <em>ren</em> (仁), a kind of deep humaneness or benevolence that should govern all our interactions. The Buddhists practice <em>metta</em>, or loving-kindness, a boundless goodwill extended to all beings, not just our chosen few. Both traditions understood that love is not a feeling you wait for, but a capacity you build. It is the practice of seeing the humanity in the person in front of you, of wishing them well for their own sake, whether they are your dearest friend or a stranger. This is the work of a lifetime. It is difficult and it is quiet, but it is the only thing I have found that makes any of our connections—fleeting or lasting, joyful or painful—truly worthwhile.
