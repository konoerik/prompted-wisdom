Sooner or later, the wave hits. It may be a small one that only knocks you off your feet for a moment, or it may be a tsunami that scours your world clean of everything you thought was permanent. No life is exempt. The first and most honest thing the great traditions have to say about suffering is that it is not a deviation from the path; it is the path. The Buddhists have a word for it, *dukkha*, which is often translated as suffering but is closer to a pervasive sense of dissatisfaction, of things being fundamentally out-of-joint. To be born is to be subject to sickness, old age, and death. This is not a pessimistic complaint but a diagnosis, the essential fact from which all thinking about how to live must begin.

For decades, I have watched this truth play out, and I have found no better starting point than the one offered, in remarkably similar terms, by both the Stoics of ancient Rome and the early Buddhists. The insight is this: the primary source of our suffering is not the event itself, but our judgment about the event. The philosopher Epictetus, a man who was born a slave and knew suffering intimately, said it as plainly as it can be said: “Men are disturbed not by things, but by the views which they take of them.” A job is lost. A relationship ends. A diagnosis is given. These are facts, like rocks in a field. It is the story we tell ourselves about these facts—that this is a catastrophe, that our life is over, that we have failed—that creates the anguish.

This is where the work of resilience begins. It is an internal art. The Stoics built their entire ethics on a simple, fiercely practical distinction: some things are up to us, and some things are not. What is not up to us? Our bodies, our reputations, our possessions, the actions of others, the course of the world. In short, almost everything we spend our lives trying to control. What *is* up to us? Our thoughts, our judgments, our intentions, our will. That is the whole list. The task, then, is to train your mind to focus its energy solely on that tiny domain you command, and to cultivate an attitude of acceptance—not passive resignation, but a calm, clear-eyed recognition of reality—toward everything else. Marcus Aurelius, an emperor who lived this philosophy at the pinnacle of power, wrote in his private journal, “The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way.” The obstacle is not something to be removed so you can get on with your life. The obstacle *is* your life.

This is a difficult thought, I know. It can feel like a cold comfort when the heart is breaking. But I have found it to be an anchor in the worst storms. It gives you a place to stand. From that firm ground, you can look toward an even more demanding idea, one articulated with bracing force by Friedrich Nietzsche. He called it *amor fati*: the love of one’s fate. This is not just the acceptance of suffering, but the *embrace* of it. It is the desire that everything in your life, the beautiful and the terrible, should be exactly as it is. To be a creator, Nietzsche argued, one must be able to affirm the totality of one’s existence, including its most painful moments, because they are what forged you. “What does not kill me,” he wrote, “makes me stronger.” It is a fearsome thought, but it contains a wild and powerful freedom: the power to look your own life in the eye and say, *Yes. All of it.*

Perhaps this feels too grand. There is a quieter, more modern version of this strength in the work of Albert Camus. He asks us to consider the Greek myth of Sisyphus, condemned by the gods to push a boulder up a mountain for eternity, only to watch it roll back down each time. Camus sees this as a metaphor for the human condition: a life of repetitive, absurd labor. Where is the hope in that? The hope, Camus says, is in the moment Sisyphus walks back down the mountain to begin again. In that moment, he is conscious of his fate. He is not a victim of it; he is superior to it. By choosing to find his purpose in the struggle itself, he robs the gods of their victory. “The struggle itself toward the heights is enough to fill a man’s heart,” Camus concludes. “One must imagine Sisyphus happy.”

Across these traditions, a single truth emerges. Resilience is not the absence of suffering; it is the discovery of meaning within it. It is the space we carve out between the blow and our response. It is the quiet, stubborn, and deeply human power to choose what a thing will mean. This power cannot stop the waves from coming, but it can teach you how to stand your ground when they hit. And in the dignity of that stance, you may find you have everything you need.
