You did not choose the world you were born into, any more than you chose your own parents. You arrived in the middle of a story that had been running for millennia, a vast and intricate play where the stage was set, the roles were outlined, and the dialogue was already in motion. Your first task was simply to learn your lines. This is the fundamental, unshakeable fact of our existence: we are social creatures, thrown into a web of relationships, obligations, and expectations not of our own making.

The question, then, is not whether to be a part of society, but how. For much of human history, the answer was clear. The ancients, almost without exception, saw us as beings defined by our communities. To be a person was to be a citizen, a son, a wife, a neighbor. Aristotle’s famous declaration that man is a “political animal” is not just a description but a judgment: a person living outside the <em>polis</em>, the city-state, was either a beast or a god, but not fully human. The good life could only be achieved with and among others. The Confucians went further, building an entire ethical system on the foundation of our relationships. For them, you are not an isolated self who then decides to interact with others; you are constituted by your role as a parent, a child, a subject, a friend. Your virtue lies in performing that role with sincerity and grace.

I have found this to be a hard but necessary truth. The modern dream of the purely self-made individual, beholden to no one, is a fantasy. It has no precedent in the long history of human thought because it has no basis in human reality. We are shaped by the language we speak, the customs we inherit, the laws we obey, and the thousand invisible threads of connection that hold us upright. The Roman emperor Marcus Aurelius, a man who sat at the very pinnacle of society, saw this with perfect clarity. He reminded himself constantly of his connection to the whole, writing in his private notes, “What is not good for the hive is not good for the bee.” This is a line I have carried with me for decades. It is a simple, profound anchor. Your own good, in the final analysis, cannot be disentangled from the good of the human community to which you belong.

This is not to say you must accept the world as you find it. The Buddhist ideal of the Bodhisattva offers a different kind of engagement. Here is a being who, on the verge of attaining final enlightenment, chooses to turn back, to remain in the world of suffering out of boundless compassion for all others. This is not a duty born of social contract, but an active choice born of empathy. It suggests that your place in society is not just to fulfill a role, but to ease the burdens of those around you. It transforms obligation into an act of love.

And yet, we have all felt the friction. We have all felt the judgment, the pressure to conform, the sting of being misunderstood. Jean-Paul Sartre’s famous line, “Hell is other people,” resonates for a reason. He was not saying that people are inherently evil, but that the unblinking gaze of another can feel like a prison, fixing you in place, reducing your infinite possibilities to a single, static definition. Society can feel like a cage as much as a home.

So where does this leave you? Caught between the ancient truth that you cannot live without others and the modern agony that you sometimes cannot live with them. I can only tell you what has seemed true to me. The thinkers who insisted on our social nature were right. There is no flourishing in isolation. But your participation does not have to be passive. The hive needs the bee, but it is still your task to make your own honey. Your work is to find the narrow path between resentful rebellion and blind conformity. It is to understand the rules of the world you were given, and then to decide, consciously and with intention, which ones you will live by, which you will challenge, and which you will rewrite through your own example. You are a part of the web, that is certain. Your task is not to escape it, but to learn to be a thoughtful weaver.
