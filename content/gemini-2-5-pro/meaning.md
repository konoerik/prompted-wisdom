The question of meaning is a heavy one. It feels vast and abstract, the kind of question you ask the stars on a clear night and expect only silence in return. I have found that asking "What is the meaning of life?" is often the wrong way to begin. It is a question that looks for a single, universal answer, as if it were a secret password to be discovered. But life is not a locked room. A better question, a more practical one, is how you can live a meaningful life. The answer to that is not found in the stars, but in the world right in front of you.

The world, some thinkers of the last century concluded, does not hand you a script. You are born, and then you must choose. This can feel like a terrifying freedom, a kind of abandonment. But in it lies a profound power. If there is no pre-written meaning, then you are the author. The French writer Albert Camus saw this in the old Greek myth of Sisyphus, the king condemned by the gods to push a boulder up a hill for eternity, only to watch it roll back down each time. A meaningless task. Yet Camus ends his essay with a startling line: "One must imagine Sisyphus happy." Why? Because in the thick of the struggle, in the full-throated effort of pushing the stone, Sisyphus finds his purpose. The meaning is not in reaching the summit, but in the dignity of the effort itself. I have seen this truth play out again and again. Meaning is forged in the doing, not in the destination.

This is a modern idea, born of a world where the old certainties had crumbled. The ancients, however, offered a different kind of anchor. For the Stoics, meaning was not something you created from scratch, but something you aligned yourself with. It was about understanding your nature as a rational, social being and then living in accordance with it. The emperor Marcus Aurelius, writing in his tent during a military campaign, asked himself a simple question that I have carried with me for years: "What is your art? To be a good man." That was it. The purpose of a doctor is to heal, the purpose of a builder is to build, and the purpose of a human being is to be good—to be just, temperate, courageous, and wise. Meaning was found in the excellence of your own character, in fulfilling your duties to your community not because you were commanded to, but because it was the rational and right thing to do.

These two poles—meaning as something you create and meaning as something you fulfill—are not as far apart as they seem. The psychiatrist Viktor Frankl, who survived the Nazi concentration camps, saw how they could meet. He watched men who had lost everything—their families, their homes, their dignity—and observed that the ones who survived were often those who held on to a sense of meaning. He concluded that we don't invent meaning out of thin air, nor do we simply discover it like a lost object. We find it. We find it in three places: in creating a work or doing a deed; in loving another person; and in the attitude we take toward unavoidable suffering. Even in the worst of circumstances, he realized, you still have the last of the human freedoms: to choose your own way, to find meaning in your response to what life has given you.

The Buddhist tradition takes this a step further. It suggests that the intense search for a personal meaning, for <em>my</em> purpose, can itself be a source of suffering. The focus shifts from the individual self to the interconnectedness of all things. Meaning, in this view, arises not from what you achieve for yourself, but from your compassion for others. It is found in the recognition that your well-being is inseparable from the well-being of everyone else. The goal is not to build a monument to yourself, but to dissolve the walls that separate you from the world.

So where does this leave you? Without a single, simple answer, I am afraid. But perhaps with something more solid. Meaning is not a revelation; it is a practice. It is what you find in the dedicated work of your hands and mind. It is what you feel in the unwavering love you give to another. It is the courage you show when faced with pain you cannot escape. And it is the quiet compassion that connects you to the person next to you. It is not one thing, but all of these things. It is the anchor you build for yourself, day by day, out of the stuff of your own life.
