Virtue and character have been the cornerstones of philosophical inquiry for centuries, a testament to their enduring relevance in our quest to understand what it means to live a good life. The ancient Greeks, particularly Aristotle, laid the groundwork for much of our thinking on these subjects. Aristotle posited that virtue is a habit, a disposition to act in ways that benefit both the individual and society. He saw virtue as a mean between extremes, a balance that requires wisdom and discernment. This idea of balance, of finding the golden mean, is echoed across cultures and philosophies.

The Stoics, contemporaries of Aristotle, took a different approach. For them, virtue was the only true good, and it was sufficient for happiness. They believed that external circumstances were beyond our control and thus irrelevant to our moral character. What mattered was how we responded to these circumstances. This emphasis on internal control and resilience resonates with Buddhist teachings, which also stress the importance of inner peace and detachment from worldly desires. Both traditions teach that true strength comes from within, from a character forged in the fires of adversity.

As I walked through bustling markets and sat at kitchen tables around the world, I saw these ideas reflected in the lives of ordinary people. Yet, she spoke of her misfortune with a calm acceptance that reminded me of Epictetus, the Stoic philosopher who taught that we should focus on what we can control and let go of what we cannot. Her resilience was a testament to the power of character, a living embodiment of the Stoic ideal.

The Confucians, too, have much to say about virtue and character. Confucius emphasized the importance of relationships and the role of virtue in maintaining harmony within them. He believed that a person of good character acts with benevolence, righteousness, propriety, and wisdom. These virtues are cultivated through education and self-reflection, a process that requires both discipline and humility. Confucius's teachings remind us that character is not just an individual pursuit but a social one, deeply intertwined with our responsibilities to others.

Montaigne, writing in the Renaissance, picked up these threads and wove them into his essays. He was skeptical of grand systems and absolute truths, preferring instead to explore the complexities of human nature. For Montaigne, virtue was not about adhering to rigid rules but about understanding oneself and one's place in the world. He believed that character is revealed in how we handle life's uncertainties, a view that aligns with the existentialists who followed him, like Nietzsche and Frankl.

Nietzsche challenged traditional notions of virtue, arguing that they were often tools of oppression. He called for a reevaluation of values, urging individuals to create their own moral frameworks. This idea of self-creation, of forging one's own path, is a powerful reminder that character is not static but dynamic, shaped by our choices and experiences. Frankl, a Holocaust survivor, echoed this sentiment in his writings on finding meaning in suffering. He believed that even in the direst circumstances, we have the freedom to choose our attitude, to find purpose and dignity in our actions.
